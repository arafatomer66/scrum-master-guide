# LeSS (Large-Scale Scrum)

*Bas Vodde and Craig Larman's scaling framework, built on a deliberately provocative premise: scale by removing organizational complexity, not by adding process.*

Large-Scale Scrum (LeSS) is the outlier among the major scaling frameworks because its central move is subtractive. Where Nexus adds a role and extended events, and SAFe adds an entire layer of roles, artifacts, and cadences, LeSS's founding question is: what is the absolute minimum we need to add to standard Scrum to make it work with multiple teams — and how much of what looks like "necessary complexity" in a large organization is actually just organizational debt that should be removed instead of managed?

## "More with LeSS"

LeSS's tagline, "more with LeSS," is not just wordplay — it states the framework's actual thesis. Descaling — removing unnecessary organizational complexity, roles, hand-offs, and specialist silos — is treated as the primary scaling strategy, and adding coordination process is treated as the last resort, not the first move. Larman's Laws of Organizational Behavior (a set of observations Craig Larman published alongside LeSS) capture the cynicism behind this: organizations are implicitly optimized to avoid changing their existing power structures, middle management and specialist job titles will tend to reappear under new names even after a reorg, and any change initiative will be reinterpreted to mean whatever lets the status quo continue with minimum actual disruption. LeSS is designed specifically to resist that gravity by refusing to give the organization new roles and titles to hide behind.

Practically, this means a LeSS adoption often starts by asking uncomfortable questions a lighter-touch framework never raises: why does this organization have a separate QA department instead of testers embedded in teams? Why are there five component teams that must all touch a single feature instead of a handful of feature teams that could each build it independently? Why does this project need a Business Analyst layer translating between the PO and the Developers? LeSS's answer to most of these is: remove the layer, don't formalize a process for coordinating around it.

## LeSS vs. LeSS Huge

LeSS comes in two sizes, and the distinction matters because they solve for different failure modes:

- **LeSS (basic).** Up to roughly 8 teams (roughly 50 people) working from **one Product Backlog**, with **one Product Owner**, and **one Definition of Done** shared across every team, all Sprinting in the same cadence. There is no additional management structure between the PO and the teams — the PO prioritizes the single backlog, and teams pull directly from it, self-selecting or being assigned items in a way that keeps them cross-functional and independent.
- **LeSS Huge.** For organizations beyond roughly 8 teams, where a single Product Owner genuinely cannot maintain the requisite context and stakeholder relationships across the whole backlog. LeSS Huge introduces **Requirement Areas** — the product is divided into large areas of customer/business concern (not technical components), each with its own **Area Product Owner** who reports into the overall PO and manages a sub-backlog scoped to that area. Multiple teams work within a Requirement Area, still from one Product Backlog for that area, still one Definition of Done. This is LeSS's only concession to genuinely large scale, and it's introduced as late as possible, only once basic LeSS's single-PO model has demonstrably stopped working.

Both variants keep every other Scrum accountability, event, and artifact exactly as the Scrum Guide defines them — LeSS does not rename Sprint Planning, does not invent a parallel Daily Scrum ritual layered on top, and does not introduce a Scrum Master role that differs from the standard one. Coordination between teams in LeSS happens primarily through informal mechanisms LeSS actively cultivates — a shared open-space-style planning session, cross-team pairing, a "Scrum of Scrums"-style optional standup a team can call if needed, communities of practice — rather than through mandated ceremonies with fixed names and fixed attendee lists.

## Single Product Backlog, single PO, single Definition of Done

The three artifacts LeSS insists must be unified across every team, regardless of team count, are the backbone of the entire framework:

- **One Product Backlog.** All teams pull from the same prioritized list — there is no team-specific backlog, no component backlog. This is what forces teams toward feature-team structure rather than component-team structure: if there's only one backlog and it's organized around customer value, a team optimized to build only one architectural layer can't cleanly pull items off it.
- **One Product Owner.** Even with 8 teams pulling from it, LeSS insists on a single PO for the whole product (in basic LeSS) specifically to prevent the fragmented, locally-optimized prioritization that emerges the moment each team gets its own backlog and its own priorities.
- **One Definition of Done**, shared by every team, applied to every Increment regardless of which team produced it. A feature isn't "done for Team A's part" and separately "done for Team B's part" — it's done, period, against one bar every team is held to.

## Why LeSS deliberately avoids new roles and artifacts

This is the sharpest contrast with Nexus (which adds the Nexus Integration Team role) and especially with SAFe (which adds Release Train Engineer, Solution Train Engineer, System Architect, Epic Owner, and more). LeSS's position is that every new role added to manage scale is itself a form of complexity that then needs to be coordinated, staffed, and eventually defended by whoever now holds that title — which is precisely the dynamic Larman's Laws predict will calcify into permanent bureaucracy. Instead of adding a "Scaled Scrum Master" or an "Integration Team," LeSS asks existing Scrum Masters to cover multiple teams (commonly 1-3 teams per Scrum Master) and pushes the coordination work that a dedicated integration role would otherwise own back onto the teams themselves, supported by structural choices — feature teams, a shared Definition of Done, communities of practice — that reduce how much coordination is needed in the first place, rather than roles that manage coordination after the fact.

This is also why LeSS is often the hardest of the major frameworks to adopt in an already-large, already-hierarchical organization, and the frameworks aren't really competing for the same buyer: LeSS demands org redesign (dissolving component teams into feature teams, removing management layers, giving up separate QA/BA departments) as a precondition, not an optional extra, whereas [SAFe](safe-overview.md) explicitly maps onto the existing org structure a large enterprise already has. An organization unwilling to touch its reporting lines or job titles will find LeSS nearly impossible to adopt honestly — see [Why and When to Scale](why-and-when-to-scale.md) for how Conway's Law explains why that resistance is exactly what LeSS is trying to break.

For the argument on why real cross-team dependency (rather than headcount or org optics) is the only legitimate reason to reach for a framework like this at all, see [Why and When to Scale](why-and-when-to-scale.md); for the lighter-touch alternative that adds a small amount of structure instead of removing organizational structure, see [Nexus](nexus.md).
