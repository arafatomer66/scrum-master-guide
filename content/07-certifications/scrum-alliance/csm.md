# Certified ScrumMaster (CSM)

*Scrum Alliance's entry-level credential — the most widely recognized Scrum Master certification by sheer market presence, and the one whose real gate is a paid course, not the exam that follows it.*

## Who It's For

Developers, testers, project managers, and product people making a first formal move into the Scrum Master role, especially in North America where CSM shows up in more job postings than any other Scrum Master credential simply because it has been around the longest and Scrum Alliance has the largest installed base of trainers. It's also the right starting point for anyone whose employer is already Scrum Alliance-aligned — a company that sent its last three Scrum Masters through CSM will keep sending people through CSM, because the internal vocabulary, the trainer relationships, and the renewal tracking are already built around it. If you're choosing your first certification with no organizational pressure either way, it's worth reading this page alongside [PSM I](../scrum-org/psm-i.md) before deciding, since the two credentials test very different things despite covering the same underlying framework.

## Format & Logistics

CSM requires attending a mandatory two-day (16-hour) course taught live — in person or live-virtual — by a Certified Scrum Trainer (CST). There is no self-study path and no way to sit the exam without first completing the course; this is the single fact that defines everything else about the certification. Course fees vary by trainer, region, and format, but commonly land somewhere in the neighborhood of $1,000–$1,500 USD; treat that as a rough band, not a quote, and confirm current pricing directly with the CST or training provider before you register.

After the course, Scrum Alliance grants exam access: an online, multiple-choice, open-book test you can take on your own schedule within a set window after the course ends. The question count, time limit, and passing threshold have changed more than once over the years — historically it has been in the range of 50 questions with a passing score around 74% — so treat any specific number you read (including this one) as approximate and verify the current exam format on Scrum Alliance's site before you sit it. You typically get a couple of free attempts within the registration window; additional retakes beyond that usually carry a small fee.

Passing earns a two-year certification. Renewal requires accumulating Scrum Education Units (SEUs) — continuing-education credit earned through further training, conferences, volunteering, or coaching — plus a renewal fee, again best confirmed at renewal time rather than assumed from an old number. Let the certification lapse without renewing and it goes inactive; Scrum Alliance does not treat it as permanent.

## What It Actually Tests

Officially, the exam checks basic fluency with Scrum's roles, events, artifacts, and values — material drawn straight from the Scrum Guide and reinforced during the course. In practice, the exam is not the hard part. Because it's taken immediately after 16 hours of live instruction on exactly the material it covers, and because the format is open-book with generous attempt allowances, CSM has a reputation — earned, not exaggerated — for being fairly easy to pass once you've sat through the course. The real gate is upstream: paying for and attending the course itself. This is a deliberate design choice by Scrum Alliance, not an accident — the organization's model is built on live human instruction as the primary transmission mechanism for Scrum, with the exam functioning more as a completion check than a filter.

That design has a direct consequence for what the credential actually signals. A CSM tells an employer "this person paid for and sat through two days of live Scrum training with a certified trainer." It does not, by itself, tell them "this person has demonstrated independent mastery of the Scrum Guide under exam pressure" — that claim belongs more to [PSM I](../scrum-org/psm-i.md), which has no mandatory course and a genuinely harder, timed, proctored exam that people do fail. Neither framing is wrong; they're measuring different things, and it's worth being honest with yourself about which one you're actually getting before you register.

## Study Plan

Because the course is the real content delivery mechanism and the exam is comparatively light, the highest-leverage thing you can do before registering is not cramming — it's choosing a good trainer. CST quality varies enormously. Some run interactive, simulation-heavy workshops with real facilitation practice, small cohorts, and exercises built around actual team dysfunction; others run a slide deck for sixteen hours with a multiple-choice quiz at the end. Look for trainers with a track record of hands-on facilitation exercises, reviews that mention actually practicing skills rather than sitting through lecture, and — ideally — trainers who have recently worked as practicing Scrum Masters themselves, not just as full-time trainers.

Before the course, read the Scrum Guide once, cold, so the vocabulary — Sprint Goal, Definition of Done, the three artifacts, the five events — is already familiar when the trainer introduces it. This matters less for passing the exam and more for what you get out of the sixteen hours: if you're not spending class time learning what a Sprint Backlog is for the first time, you can spend it on the parts a document can't teach you, like running a Daily Scrum that doesn't turn into a status meeting or facilitating a Retrospective for a team that's stopped being honest with itself.

During the course itself, engage fully. Ask questions grounded in your own team's real situations rather than staying passive through the exercises. The exam afterward needs almost no separate study if you did this — the material is fresh, the format is forgiving, and the two free attempts give you room even if you stumble.

## Common Failure Points

The exam itself is rarely where CSM candidates fail — the far more common and more consequential failure happens after the certificate is issued. Because attendance is the real gate and the exam rarely blocks anyone who showed up, it's entirely possible to walk out with a CSM certificate while never having genuinely internalized why Scrum's empirical loop — transparency, inspection, adaptation — actually matters. That gap shows up later, not on a test, but in the room: a "certified" Scrum Master who defends the Daily Scrum time-box because "the Guide says fifteen minutes" without being able to explain why that time-box exists, or who can't tell a team the difference between a legitimate Sprint-length change and a Retrospective quietly getting skipped until it disappears. See [Understanding and Applying the Scrum Framework](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/01-understanding-and-applying-scrum-framework.md) for what that fluency actually looks like under pressure — it's the competency CSM's format makes easiest to skip.

This is the honest contrast worth sitting with: [PSM I](../scrum-org/psm-i.md)'s harder, self-study exam gate forces a kind of independent grappling with the Scrum Guide's exact language that CSM's course-plus-easy-exam format doesn't require of everyone who passes through it. Neither path guarantees competence — plenty of PSM I holders have never run a real Sprint, and plenty of CSMs are excellent Scrum Masters — but if you take CSM, the responsibility for closing the gap between "certified" and "actually understands empiricism" falls more on you, during and after the two days, than on the exam that follows them. Treating the weekend as a checkbox rather than the start of real practice is the single most common way people end up with the badge and not the skill.
