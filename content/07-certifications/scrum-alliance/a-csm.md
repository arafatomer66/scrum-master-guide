# Advanced Certified ScrumMaster (A-CSM)

*The credential Scrum Alliance built specifically for people who already know the mechanics and need the harder skill — reading a team, coaching instead of directing, and facilitating conflict instead of avoiding it.*

## Who It's For

A-CSM is for practicing Scrum Masters who have moved past the "what does the Scrum Guide say" phase and are hitting the ceiling that only real field time exposes — a Daily Scrum that technically happens on schedule but has gone stale, a team stuck in permanent storming that no amount of reciting Scrum theory fixes, a stakeholder relationship that needs a coaching conversation, not a status update. Scrum Alliance gates enrollment accordingly: you need an active [CSM](csm.md) plus roughly twelve months of Scrum Master experience within the last five years (confirm the current figure on Scrum Alliance's site, as eligibility requirements do get revised). That eligibility bar is doing real work here — it exists specifically to keep this from being "CSM, part two" for people who haven't yet lived the job long enough to have anything concrete to bring into the room.

## Format & Logistics

A-CSM courses are taught by trainers Scrum Alliance has specifically authorized to deliver this content — typically Certified Team Coaches (CTCs) or CSTs with A-CSM authorization — and run roughly 16 hours total, though the delivery format varies more than CSM's fixed two consecutive days: some providers run it as two full days, others as four half-day sessions spread over a couple of weeks, which for working practitioners can actually be the better format since it leaves time between sessions to try something in your own Sprint and bring the result back to class.

Unlike CSM, there is no separate proctored exam. Completion is assessed through a combination of in-course participation and post-course learning objectives — a set of reflective assignments or demonstrated behaviors the trainer signs off on — rather than a multiple-choice test. Cost tends to run somewhat higher than CSM, commonly in a rough $1,000–$1,600 USD band depending on trainer and format; verify current pricing with the provider rather than relying on that range. Certification lasts two years and renews through SEUs — typically a higher SEU requirement than CSM's — plus a renewal fee; confirm the exact current figures at renewal time, since Scrum Alliance updates these periodically.

## What It Actually Tests

There's no exam to describe here in the usual sense, and that's the point: A-CSM isn't testing more Scrum trivia, it's building — and then checking for — applied skill in the areas CSM barely touches. Typical course content covers facilitation techniques beyond the basic event agendas, coaching stances versus directive management, situational leadership adjusted to a team's maturity, conflict resolution and team-dynamics models (many trainers use Tuckman's stages — forming, storming, norming, performing — as a working frame), and service to the broader organization rather than just the one team. Some providers offer A-CSM with a specialty lean toward coaching, facilitation, or organizational service; the core learning objectives are set by Scrum Alliance, but emphasis varies by trainer.

The assessment, such as it is, comes from whether you can actually do these things in the room during the course — run a real facilitation exercise, receive and act on feedback about your own facilitation style, work through a conflict scenario using more than "let's just talk it out." A trainer signing off on your learning objectives is a judgment call about demonstrated skill, not a pass/fail line on a scored test.

## Study Plan

Prepping for A-CSM by reviewing the Scrum Guide again is close to wasted effort — the course assumes that knowledge is already solid and spends its time somewhere else entirely. The higher-leverage preparation is going in with real material from your own team: a Retrospective that produced nothing actionable, a stakeholder who hijacked a Sprint Review, a conflict between two developers that you managed by avoiding it. Bank a handful of these before the course starts. A-CSM's coaching labs and facilitation exercises are far more useful when you're working through a situation you actually lived than a hypothetical one the trainer invented for the slide deck.

The deeper prep material worth reading beforehand lives in this guide's role-craft section rather than its framework section: [Facilitation Techniques](../../04-scrum-master-role/facilitation-techniques.md) and [Coaching vs. Mentoring vs. Teaching](../../04-scrum-master-role/coaching-vs-mentoring-vs-teaching.md) cover the exact distinctions — when to facilitate versus decide, when to coach versus simply tell someone the answer — that A-CSM spends sixteen hours building into muscle memory. Reading them first means you walk in with the vocabulary already in place and can spend class time on practice reps instead of definitions.

During the course, treat the exercises as real skill-building, not performance. The value of A-CSM comes almost entirely from feedback on your actual facilitation and coaching behavior — how you actually phrase a question, where you jump to advice when a coaching stance would serve the team better, how you read a room that's gone quiet. That feedback only works if you show up willing to be visibly imperfect in front of the trainer and your peers.

## Common Failure Points

The most common failure is enrolling too early — meeting the letter of the eligibility requirement without yet having the field scars the course assumes. Someone who got their CSM eleven months ago and has run exactly one real Sprint can technically qualify once the clock hits twelve months, but they'll sit through the course with nothing concrete to bring to the coaching labs, and the exercises will land as abstract theory instead of practice against something real. The eligibility bar is a floor, not a guarantee of readiness; if you don't yet have a genuine list of hard situations from your own team, more field time will make the course far more valuable than rushing in the moment you're technically eligible.

The second failure mode is showing up expecting more Scrum recall and getting defensive when the course instead points a mirror at your own facilitation habits. A-CSM's value is almost entirely in peer and trainer feedback on your actual behavior, and that feedback only lands if you're open to hearing that your coaching stance defaults to giving answers, or that you unconsciously dominate discussions you're supposed to be facilitating. Treating the course as a badge to collect rather than a mirror to look into wastes the format's entire design.

Finally, the same trap that catches CSM catches A-CSM at a higher altitude: mistaking attendance for skill. A genuine A-CSM can run a real values-based conflict conversation between two team members or correctly read a team stuck in storming and adjust their facilitation accordingly. A rubber-stamp A-CSM has the certificate and a vocabulary for these situations but hasn't actually built the muscle. The differentiator isn't the badge — it's whether the coaching and facilitation skill this credential is meant to certify actually shows up the next time your team hits a real conflict.
