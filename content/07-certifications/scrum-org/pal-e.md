# PAL-E — Professional Agile Leadership - Essentials

*Scrum.org's entry-level leadership credential — not a test of whether you can run a Sprint, but of whether you understand what leaders do to enable or accidentally destroy a self-managing team.*

Every other certification in this section tests knowledge that lives inside the Scrum Team — how to run the events, how to coach the team, how to manage the backlog. PAL-E tests something that lives outside it: whether the people with organizational authority over the team — managers, directors, executives, portfolio owners — understand what their own behavior does to a Scrum Team's ability to self-manage. That's a genuinely different subject, not a leadership-flavored retread of the Scrum Guide. A Scrum Team can run every event with textbook precision and still be quietly strangled by a manager who assigns individual tasks in one-on-ones, or a promotion system that rewards heroics over sustainable delivery, or a funding model that reorganizes team membership every quarter around whatever project just got approved. PAL-E exists because Scrum.org's own data and experience made clear that a huge share of "the team can't get agile to work" failures trace back to leadership behavior the team has no power to change themselves — which is also exactly the territory [Evolving the Agile Organization](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md) covers in this guide.

## Who It's For

PAL-E is for managers, directors, executives, and portfolio or program leads who sit above one or more Scrum Teams and make decisions about structure, funding, staffing, and what gets rewarded — the people whose choices set the conditions a team either thrives or struggles inside, whether or not those people ever attend a Daily Scrum. It's also a strong fit for Scrum Masters and agile coaches who've hit the ceiling described in Evolving the Agile Organization: the team itself is doing everything right, and the actual impediment sits one or two levels up the org chart, in a leader's habits or a structural decision the Scrum Master has no authority to change directly. PAL-E gives that Scrum Master a shared vocabulary and a credential that carries weight specifically with the leadership audience they need to influence — a Scrum Master waving the Scrum Guide at a VP rarely lands the same way a leadership-focused credential and framing does.

It is not for someone who wants to prove they can facilitate Scrum events or coach a team day to day — that's the [PSM line](psm-i.md), and PAL-E assumes none of that operational skill. It's also not really a credential you sit to prove something to yourself about your own team-level Scrum knowledge; it's aimed outward, at influencing people whose job isn't Scrum at all, which makes it one of the few certifications in this guide where the target audience mostly isn't Scrum Team members.

## Format & Logistics

- **Format:** Like every Scrum.org assessment, PAL-E requires no mandatory course — you can sit the exam without ever attending training. A companion "Professional Agile Leadership - Essentials" course exists and is commonly bundled by employers who are funding the learning anyway, but it's optional for the assessment itself, consistent with Scrum.org's broader philosophy of selling assessments, not gated training (see [which certification should I get](../00-which-certification-should-i-get.md) for that philosophical split in more depth). The exam itself is open-book, timed, delivered on Scrum.org's own platform, no proctor or testing center required.
- **Length and passing score:** Scrum.org has not published PAL-E's exact question count, time limit, and passing threshold with the same consistency it has for PSM I, and the leadership line's format has shifted over time. Treat it as a shorter, lighter assessment than PSM I rather than assuming the same numbers apply, and **verify the current length, time limit, and passing score directly on scrum.org before registering.**
- **Cost:** A single flat per-attempt fee paid directly to Scrum.org. **Verify the current price on scrum.org** — don't assume it matches PSM I's price tier exactly, since Scrum.org prices its credential lines somewhat independently.
- **Retake policy:** Same purchase-another-attempt model as the rest of the Scrum.org catalog — no mandatory waiting period, but repeated failures are a signal to revisit the underlying leadership concepts rather than just re-attempt.
- **Prerequisites:** None. No mandatory training, no application review, no requirement to hold any other certification first.
- **Validity:** No expiration, no renewal fee — consistent with every Scrum.org Professional-line credential.

## What It Actually Tests

PAL-E tests whether you can tell the difference between leadership behavior that creates the conditions for a self-managing team and leadership behavior that quietly undermines it, even when the person doing the undermining believes they're helping. The exam leans hard on scenarios where a well-intentioned leader does something that feels responsible — steps in to unblock a decision, sets a firm deadline to create focus, reorganizes reporting lines to fix a coordination problem — and asks whether that action actually enabled the team or just moved the authority further away from the people doing the work.

A recurring pattern worth internalizing, the leadership-level equivalent of PSM I's authority-grab trap:

- **"A good leader makes the tough calls so the team doesn't have to."** Usually wrong. The leader's job in an agile organization is to set clear outcomes and boundaries and then push decision authority about *how* down to the people closest to the work — not to keep resolving it themselves because it's faster in the moment.
- **"Restructuring the org chart is how you become agile."** Wrong on its own. Structural change without a change in what leaders actually reward, measure, and model is cosmetic — teams read what leadership does far more than what leadership announces.
- **"Individual performance reviews and forced ranking drive better results."** Wrong for self-managing teams. These systems incentivize local, individual optimization and quiet competition exactly where collaboration and shared ownership are needed most.
- **"A detailed long-range plan reduces risk."** Wrong in genuinely complex, uncertain work. Detailed upfront plans manage risk well in complicated-but-predictable domains; in complex product work, short empirical cycles — inspect, adapt, replan — manage uncertainty better than a plan leadership then resists changing once it's approved.
- **"Leaders should shield the organization from all failure."** Wrong framing. The leadership job is making failure cheap and fast to learn from — psychological safety to try something and be visibly wrong — not eliminating risk entirely, which usually eliminates the experimentation that produces real innovation along with it.
- **"A manager sitting in on the Daily Scrum keeps things honest."** A trap. A leader's presence at team-level events, even silently, tends to turn the event into a performance for the person with positional power in the room — the opposite of what the event is for. PAL-E tests whether you recognize that a leader's most useful contribution is often absence, not oversight.

The exam also draws on organizational design and culture-change territory covered in [Evolving the Agile Organization](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md) — functional silos versus stream-aligned teams, the gap between "doing agile" and "being agile" — but from the leader's vantage point specifically: what a leader does to remove a structural impediment a team cannot remove itself, and why that's the leader's accountability, not the Scrum Master's, even though the Scrum Master is often the one who first names the problem. It also introduces Evidence-Based Management at a survey level — the four key value areas that make organizational value conversations empirical instead of opinion-driven — without going anywhere near the depth [PAL-EBM](pal-ebm.md) tests; if a PAL-E question leans hard into specific EBM measures and how to apply them to a live investment decision, that's a strong signal you're actually looking at PAL-EBM content, not PAL-E's.

## Study Plan

1. **Read [Evolving the Agile Organization](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md) closely — this is the single closest match to PAL-E's actual content in this guide.** Its sections on organizational design, culture change, and the EBM overview map almost directly onto what PAL-E tests, more than any Scrum Guide reading would.
2. **Read [Servant Leadership](../../04-scrum-master-role/servant-leadership.md), then deliberately re-read it asking "how does this apply to someone with real positional authority, not just a Scrum Master with none?"** Greenleaf's original test — do those served grow more capable and autonomous — applies just as sharply to a VP as to a Scrum Master, and PAL-E scenarios are frequently built around a leader failing that test while believing they're succeeding at it.
3. **Study the leadership-behavior trap list above until your instinct flips.** Most people preparing for PAL-E still default to traditional management reflexes — resolve the ambiguity yourself, set the deadline, reward the individual who worked the weekend. The exam is built to catch exactly that reflex.
4. **Skim [section 04, The Scrum Master Role](../../04-scrum-master-role/), for context even though it's written from the Scrum Master's seat, not a leader's.** Seeing what good team-level facilitation and coaching actually look like makes it much easier to recognize, from the outside, when a leader's behavior is interfering with it.
5. **If your organization offers the official PAL-E companion course, take it as reinforcement, not as a substitute for the reading above** — it's optional for the assessment, but useful if your employer is already funding it.

## Common Failure Points

- **Treating this like PSM I's trivia recall.** PAL-E has far less exact terminology to memorize than the Scrum Guide-based exams; it rewards recognizing behavioral patterns, not recalling defined terms.
- **Confusing team-level servant leadership with organizational-level leadership.** A Scrum Master's servant leadership operates inside the team, with no positional authority. PAL-E is about people who *do* have positional authority choosing to use it in service of the team's autonomy rather than to override it — a different, harder discipline.
- **Assuming any answer that keeps decision-making centralized at the top is the "responsible" one.** It's usually the wrong one on this exam, even when it sounds prudent.
- **Missing that a leader's biggest lever is often what they stop doing.** Stopping the request for a detailed six-month plan, stopping the individual hero-rewarding, stopping the reflexive escalation-solving — PAL-E scenarios reward recognizing subtraction as a leadership move, not just new programs or policies.
- **Underestimating how much of the exam is about modeling behavior, not writing policy.** A leader who writes a beautiful agile values memo but still manages the old way in every one-on-one is the exact failure pattern several PAL-E questions are built to expose.

If you're weighing PAL-E against the Scrum Master certification track covered in the rest of this section, [which certification should I get](../00-which-certification-should-i-get.md) and the [comparison table](../comparison-table.md) walk through PSM, CSM, and SAFe in depth — but they're built around the Scrum Team's own certification path, not the leadership track. PAL-E and its deeper companion, [PAL-EBM](pal-ebm.md), sit alongside that track rather than inside it: aimed at a different audience, above the team, whose behavior the team usually can't change on its own.
