# PSM II — Professional Scrum Master II

*The intermediate Scrum.org credential — same open-book format as PSM I, a completely different kind of question.*

Professional Scrum Master II assumes you already have PSM I-level knowledge of the Scrum Guide and stops testing it directly. Instead it tests something harder to cram: whether you can apply Scrum's principles to model complex, ambiguous, real-organization situations where the "correct" move depends on judgment, not recall. If PSM I asks "what does the Scrum Guide say happens here," PSM II asks "given everything Scrum stands for, what should the Scrum Master actually do in this messy situation, where more than one answer sounds defensible and only one reflects genuinely skilled facilitation and coaching." That's a much closer match to the actual job — and to the [Professional Scrum Competencies](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/00-overview.md) model and the [real-life scenarios](../../06-real-life-scenarios/) elsewhere in this guide than it is to a second, harder pass through the Scrum Guide's text.

## Who It's For

PSM II is for Scrum Masters who've already earned PSM I (or have equivalent depth even if they skipped the credential) and have real time in the seat — running Sprint events, coaching a team through dysfunction, negotiating with a Product Owner or a skeptical manager. It rewards people who've actually lived through ambiguous situations: a team that's technically doing every Scrum event but still isn't self-managing, a Product Owner who's checked out, a stakeholder who wants a roadmap with fixed dates from a team using Scrum specifically to avoid false certainty. If PSM I proved you know the rules, PSM II is trying to prove you know what to do when the rules don't hand you a clean answer.

It is not the right next step for someone who just passed PSM I last week with zero practical experience since — you can pass PSM II on knowledge alone if you're unusually good at internalizing scenario patterns, but most people who attempt it too early fail not because they don't know Scrum, but because they haven't yet developed the pattern-matching that comes from actually facilitating real, difficult situations. Scrum.org does not enforce a prerequisite or a waiting period, but "PSM I plus real Scrum Master experience" is the honest prerequisite, not just "PSM I."

## Format & Logistics

- **Format:** Same open-book, own-platform, no-mandatory-course model as PSM I — delivered through Scrum.org's assessment platform, no proctor, no testing center, no required training class. The question types (multiple choice, multiple answer, true/false) are the same; the difference is entirely in what the questions demand of you.
- **Length:** Historically around 30 questions in roughly 90 minutes — notably fewer questions than PSM I but each one is denser: longer scenario setups, more nuance, sometimes multiple defensible-sounding options where you have to pick the *most* correct one. **Verify the current question count and time limit on scrum.org before registering** — Scrum.org periodically adjusts these.
- **Passing score:** Around 85%, consistent with Scrum.org's other Professional Scrum assessments — high enough that a handful of careless misreads can be the difference between pass and fail.
- **Cost:** A single flat per-attempt fee, higher than PSM I, paid directly to Scrum.org. **Verify the current price on scrum.org** before budgeting — do not assume it matches PSM I's price tier.
- **Retake policy:** Same as PSM I — purchase another attempt if you don't pass, no mandatory waiting period, but repeated failures are a strong signal you need more real scenario exposure, not more attempts.
- **Prerequisites:** Scrum.org does not technically require PSM I before you sit PSM II, but treating PSM II as gated behind real PSM I-equivalent knowledge (and, more importantly, real experience) is the only sane way to approach it.
- **Validity:** No expiration, no renewal fee, same as every Scrum.org Professional Scrum credential.

## What It Actually Tests

PSM II questions are almost all scenario-based, and the scenarios are deliberately underspecified in the way real situations are — you're given a team, a context, and a problem, and asked what the Scrum Master should do, with several answers that all sound plausible to someone who only knows the Scrum Guide's text. The exam is testing your ability to distinguish "technically not forbidden by the Scrum Guide" from "actually the skilled Scrum Master move," which is a much subtler line than PSM I's "the Guide explicitly says X."

Concretely, expect scenarios built around:

- **Coaching stance and facilitation judgment** — when to teach, when to coach, when to mentor, when to simply get out of the way and let the team fail safely so they learn. PSM I doesn't test this distinction at all; PSM II leans on it constantly.
- **Diagnosing organizational impediments hiding behind team-level symptoms** — a team that "can't hit its Sprint Goal" might actually be blocked by a shared-services bottleneck, a component-team structure, or a manager quietly still assigning work outside the team's visibility. PSM II wants you to see past the presenting symptom to the systemic cause.
- **Stakeholder and Product Owner dynamics** — a Product Owner who won't say no to stakeholders, a Product Owner who's absent, a stakeholder demanding date commitments Scrum's empirical process doesn't support. The correct answer usually involves the Scrum Master coaching the Product Owner or organization rather than personally intervening as a proxy Product Owner.
- **Distinguishing a legitimate Scrum adaptation from a rules violation dressed up as pragmatism** — "we skip the Daily Scrum when we're all in the same room all day anyway" sounds reasonable and is a trap; "we extended the Sprint by two days because the team asked nicely" is a clear violation with no legitimate framing.
- **Conflict and difficult conversations** — a Developer undermining another, a manager pressuring the team through the Scrum Master instead of directly, a team member who won't stop multitasking across two teams. These test facilitation and conflict-navigation judgment more than framework knowledge.
- **Metrics and transparency abuse** — velocity used to compare teams, burndown charts used to pressure individuals, a Definition of Done quietly watered down to hit a date. PSM II wants you to recognize when transparency is being gamed and know how a Scrum Master responds.

Every one of these maps directly onto the six areas in [Professional Scrum Competencies](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/00-overview.md) — particularly Developing People and Teams, and Evolving the Agile Organization — which is why that section, not the Scrum Guide's text, is the closer match for PSM II prep.

## Study Plan

The single biggest mistake people make preparing for PSM II is treating it like a harder version of PSM I prep — re-reading the Scrum Guide again, drilling more terminology, doing more open-assessment repetitions. That helps at the margin, but it is not where PSM II's difficulty actually lives. Prioritize in this order:

1. **Work through [section 04, The Scrum Master Role](../../04-scrum-master-role/), completely.** This is the material that actually maps to what PSM II tests — facilitation technique, coaching stances, metrics literacy, anti-patterns, conflict handling. If PSM I prep is "know the rulebook," PSM II prep is "know the job," and this section is where the job is taught in this guide.
2. **Read every scenario in [section 06, Real-Life Scenarios](../../06-real-life-scenarios/), and don't skip the exam-angle callouts.** Each one is structured as situation → why it happens → the Scrum Master's approach → likely outcome → certification exam angle specifically because PSM II-style questions are built the same way real ambiguous situations are. Reading twenty of these and internalizing the reasoning pattern is worth more than another read of the Scrum Guide.
3. **Re-read [Professional Scrum Competencies](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/00-overview.md) with PSM II specifically in mind**, especially Developing People and Teams and Evolving the Agile Organization — these two areas show up disproportionately often in PSM II-style scenario questions.
4. **Only after the above, take the free Scrum Open Assessment for PSM II-style practice** (Scrum.org publishes one) — useful for calibrating question difficulty and format, not for teaching you the underlying judgment, which has to come from the material above and real experience.
5. **If you're short on real-world variety, deliberately study situations outside your own team's context.** PSM II scenarios aren't all "your kind of team" — they cover component teams, distributed teams, teams new to Scrum, mature teams, teams inside SAFe-flavored organizations. If your own experience is narrow, the scenario files in this guide are the fastest way to broaden it before the exam does it for you.

## Common Failure Points

- **Picking the technically-defensible answer over the skilled-Scrum-Master answer.** PSM II often gives you an option that isn't a Scrum Guide violation but also isn't what a genuinely good Scrum Master would do — jumping in to solve a team's problem for them instead of coaching them to solve it themselves is the classic version of this trap.
- **Answering as a manager, not a servant-leader-in-practice.** Any answer where the Scrum Master directively fixes the team's process, mandates a specific technical practice, or unilaterally resolves a conflict for the team (rather than facilitating the team resolving it) is usually the wrong answer, even when it would "work."
- **Missing the systemic cause and treating the symptom.** A scenario that looks like a facilitation problem is often actually an organizational-design problem (shared resources, component teams, a manager working around the Scrum Master). Answers that only address the surface symptom tend to be the tempting-but-wrong option.
- **Under-preparing on Product Ownership and stakeholder dynamics** because it feels like "not my job" — PSM II tests how a Scrum Master coaches and supports Product Ownership and stakeholder engagement even though those are Product Owner accountabilities, because in real organizations the Scrum Master is frequently the one who has to notice and address dysfunction there.
- **Treating this as crammable the way PSM I is.** PSM I rewards focused short-term study; PSM II rewards accumulated judgment. If you sit it after a week of cramming with no real facilitation experience behind you, expect the scenario questions to feel like there are two right answers — because to someone without the experience, there sometimes genuinely do.

After PSM II, [PSM III](psm-iii.md) is the final tier — and it's a different kind of challenge entirely, one that study alone doesn't really solve.
