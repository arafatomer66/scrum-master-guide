# PSD — Professional Scrum Developer

*Scrum.org's one certification with a real training-attendance gate — you cannot self-study your way to sitting this exam the way you can for PSM or PSPO.*

Professional Scrum Developer is structurally different from every other credential in Scrum.org's catalog, and that difference is the first thing to understand about it, before what it tests. PSM I, PSM II, PSM III, and the PSPO tiers are all pure self-study assessments — read the material, take an open-book exam whenever you're ready, no course required. PSD breaks that pattern: to sit the PSD assessment, you have to first attend a licensed Professional Scrum Developer course, taught by a Scrum.org-authorized trainer, typically run as an intensive multi-day class built around a real, evolving codebase and hands-on kata-style exercises rather than lecture slides. You don't get to skip the class and go straight to the exam the way you can with every other Scrum.org credential covered in this guide. That single structural fact should shape your entire approach to PSD — it is genuinely the most directly relevant Scrum.org certification for someone who writes code day-to-day, but it's also the one you cannot simply read your way into.

## Who It's For

PSD is for Developers — in the Scrum Guide's accountability sense, covering engineers, testers, and anyone doing hands-on build work on a Scrum Team — who want a credential that specifically validates technical Scrum practice, not just framework knowledge. It's aimed at people who already write code for a living and want to prove they can apply Scrum's empirical, iterative approach to the actual mechanics of building software: emergent design instead of big upfront architecture, continuous integration instead of long-lived branches, test-driven development instead of testing as an afterthought, and disciplined management of technical debt as an ongoing practice rather than a cleanup sprint that never comes.

It is not the right fit for someone in a Scrum Master or Product Owner accountability looking for a credential — PSM and PSPO exist for exactly those tracks, and PSD's content assumes you're doing the actual coding, not facilitating or ordering a backlog. It's also not a good fit for someone hoping to avoid a live training commitment; if the appeal of Scrum.org's model is "no mandatory course," PSD is the one credential in this catalog that doesn't deliver on that. See [Developers in The Scrum Team](../../02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md) for the accountability PSD is built around before deciding it's your next certification.

## Format & Logistics

- **Format:** Unlike every other credential in this section, PSD requires attending a licensed Professional Scrum Developer course before you're eligible to sit the assessment. The course is hands-on by design — expect a real, evolving codebase, kata-style exercises done in pairs or small groups, and instruction built around actually practicing TDD, refactoring, and continuous integration during class, not just discussing them. The assessment itself, once you're eligible, follows Scrum.org's usual open-book, own-platform, multiple choice/multiple answer model.
- **Course length:** Typically run as an intensive multi-day class (commonly structured around three days), delivered live, in person or virtually, by a Scrum.org-authorized Professional Scrum Trainer with technical credentials specifically for this course. **Verify current course length, delivery format, and available trainers on scrum.org** — this varies more than PSM/PSPO's exam logistics because it's a live training product, not a pure self-serve exam.
- **Assessment length and passing score:** Broadly consistent with Scrum.org's other Level I assessments in style, though exact question count and time limit are set alongside the course and can vary. **Verify current assessment length and passing score on scrum.org before assuming it matches PSM I/PSPO I exactly.**
- **Cost:** Meaningfully higher than PSM or PSPO, because you're paying for a live, instructor-led, multi-day course plus the assessment attempt, not just an exam fee. **Verify current course and assessment pricing on scrum.org and with the specific licensed trainer or training provider** — this is the one Scrum.org credential where price varies significantly by provider, region, and delivery format, closer to how Scrum Alliance and SAFe course pricing works than to PSM I's flat exam fee.
- **Retake policy:** If you don't pass the assessment, you can purchase another attempt without repeating the course. **Verify current retake terms on scrum.org.**
- **Prerequisites:** No formal experience requirement, but you cannot sit the assessment without completing the licensed course first — that's a hard gate, not a recommendation. Come in already comfortable writing code; the course assumes baseline programming fluency and spends its time on the Scrum-specific technical practices, not on teaching you to code.
- **Validity:** Does not expire, no renewal fee, consistent with Scrum.org's other Professional Scrum credentials — the training gate is a one-time entry requirement, not an ongoing one.

## What It Actually Tests

PSD tests technical Scrum practice specifically from inside the Developers' accountability — how a team applies Scrum's empirical approach to the actual work of writing, testing, integrating, and evolving software, not the framework's events and artifacts in the abstract. Where PSM asks about facilitation and PSPO asks about value maximization, PSD asks: given Scrum's insistence that software gets built through short, empirical cycles rather than upfront design, what technical practices actually make that possible, and can you apply them, not just describe them.

Concretely, expect the course and assessment to cover:

- **Test-driven development** — writing a failing test first, the minimum code to pass it, then refactoring, as the concrete mechanism that makes emergent design safe. PSD treats TDD as a practiced skill, not a slide you memorize; the course's kata exercises exist specifically to make you do it under time pressure on a real codebase.
- **Continuous integration** — merging small, frequent changes into a shared mainline rather than accumulating long-lived branches, and understanding why CI is what keeps an Increment genuinely integrated and releasable every Sprint rather than integrated in a scramble at the end.
- **Refactoring as a continuous discipline** — improving a design's internal structure without changing its external behavior, done constantly as understanding grows, rather than treated as a separate "refactoring sprint" that competes with feature work and therefore never actually happens.
- **Emergent design** — designing for what's known now, kept cheap to change, instead of front-loading architecture decisions before any real feedback exists. This is the technical practice that makes Scrum's complex-work framing (see [Emergent Software Development](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/04-developing-and-delivering-products-professionally.md)) actually operational rather than theoretical.
- **Managing technical debt** — recognizing that a shortcut taken under Sprint pressure compounds like financial debt, and building the habits (a real Definition of Done, disciplined refactoring, honest inspection of code quality at the Sprint Review) that keep debt visible and managed instead of silently accumulating until it stalls a team a year later.
- **Applying Scrum specifically from the Developers' accountability** — self-managing how the work gets done, holding each other accountable as professionals, adapting the Sprint Backlog daily toward the Sprint Goal, and instilling quality by actually adhering to the team's Definition of Done rather than treating it as a checklist filled in after the fact.

For the conceptual grounding behind all of this — why complex work has to emerge rather than be designed upfront, why technical debt is a whole-team risk and not just an engineering inconvenience, and where a Scrum Master's advocacy for engineering discipline ends and the Developers' technical authority begins — [Developing and Delivering Products Professionally](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/04-developing-and-delivering-products-professionally.md) covers exactly this ground. Read it as preparation for the *thinking* behind PSD; it will not, by itself, prepare you for the hands-on assessment, which is the whole point of the mandatory course.

## Study Plan

PSD's preparation looks different from every other certification in this guide, because reading alone cannot get you there.

1. **Get genuinely fluent in TDD, refactoring, and CI before the course, not during it.** The licensed course assumes you can already write code comfortably and spends its time on applying these practices inside a Scrum context, not teaching you to program. If TDD is new to you, practice it on your own codebase or through kata exercises (a code kata site or book, done repeatedly, is a legitimate way to build the muscle memory) well before you sit in the class — showing up without this fluency means spending the course catching up on mechanics instead of absorbing the Scrum-specific framing.
2. **Read [Developing and Delivering Products Professionally](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/04-developing-and-delivering-products-professionally.md) before the course**, so the conceptual vocabulary — emergent design, managing technical risk, the Definition of Done as a transparency mechanism — is already familiar when the trainer uses it, rather than being introduced cold alongside a live coding exercise.
3. **Register for a licensed Professional Scrum Developer course through a Scrum.org-authorized trainer.** This is the actual, non-negotiable core of PSD preparation — verify the trainer's authorization and the course's current curriculum directly on scrum.org, since course quality and depth can vary by provider even though the credential and licensing standard are Scrum.org's.
4. **Treat the course itself as the primary study material, not a formality before self-study.** Do the kata exercises seriously, ask the trainer to connect each technical practice back to Scrum's empiricism explicitly if they don't do it unprompted, and don't let the multi-day format tempt you into passive attendance.
5. **Review [The Scrum Team](../../02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md) and [The Product Backlog](../../02-scrum-framework/product-backlog.md) for accountability boundaries** — PSD assessment questions test whether you understand where the Developers' authority over "how" ends and the Product Owner's authority over "what" and "why" begins, the same boundary tested from the other side by PSPO.
6. **After the course, take the assessment while the material is still fresh** rather than waiting weeks — because the exam draws heavily on what you just practiced hands-on, the gap between course and assessment matters more here than it does for PSM or PSPO, where the material is stable, referenceable text you can revisit anytime.

## Common Failure Points

- **Assuming strong coding skill alone is sufficient.** PSD's hardest trap for genuinely good developers is that being a strong engineer doesn't automatically mean you understand the *Scrum* framing of technical practice. A developer who's excellent at TDD but has never thought about why the Definition of Done exists as a transparency mechanism — not just a quality gate, but the thing that keeps a Sprint Review's inspection honest — will misread questions that hinge on that distinction.
- **Treating the Definition of Done as a checklist instead of a transparency mechanism.** This is worth naming specifically because it's the single most common conceptual gap: a DoD isn't there to make sure the code is good (though it does that too); it's there so that when the team calls something "Done," everyone inspecting it — the Product Owner, stakeholders, the team itself next Sprint — can trust that word means the same thing every time. A quietly watered-down DoD isn't just a quality problem, it's an empiricism problem, because every decision made from a "Done" increment that isn't really done inherits false confidence.
- **Showing up to the licensed course without baseline TDD/CI fluency.** The course is not designed to teach programming or introduce these practices from zero — arriving without hands-on comfort in them means spending expensive, limited class time catching up on mechanics instead of absorbing the Scrum-specific application the course actually teaches.
- **Underestimating that this is a real training-attendance gate, not a formality.** Some candidates approach PSD the way they'd approach PSM or PSPO — expecting to read a summary of what the course covers and sit the exam. That path doesn't exist for PSD; the course is mandatory, licensed, and hands-on, and skipping genuine engagement with it is the fastest way to be underprepared for the assessment that follows.
- **Forgetting the accountability boundary and answering like a Product Owner or Scrum Master.** PSD questions are angled from inside the Developers' accountability — self-managing the "how," instilling quality, adapting the plan daily. An answer that has a Developer unilaterally deciding what to build (a Product Owner call) or that hands authority over technical practice to someone outside the Developers (a Scrum Master or manager overstepping) is wrong regardless of how reasonable it sounds.

PSD stands apart from the rest of Scrum.org's catalog covered in this guide, and it's worth weighing that structural difference — mandatory course, hands-on assessment, higher cost — against your own goals using [which certification should I get](../00-which-certification-should-i-get.md) and the [comparison table](../comparison-table.md) before committing. If you're a Developer who wants the conceptual grounding first without registering for a course yet, [Developing and Delivering Products Professionally](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/04-developing-and-delivering-products-professionally.md) is the right place to start.
