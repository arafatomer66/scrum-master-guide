# PAL-EBM — Professional Agile Leadership - Evidence-Based Management

*Scrum.org's leadership credential built entirely around one model — Evidence-Based Management — and around the specific, harder skill of using it to make an organization's value delivery visible to people who'd otherwise decide by opinion or seniority.*

Where [PAL-E](pal-e.md) surveys the whole territory of leadership behavior in an agile organization — culture, structure, what leaders should stop doing — PAL-EBM plants its flag on one piece of that territory and goes deep: Evidence-Based Management, Scrum.org's framework for replacing opinion-driven value conversations with evidence-driven ones. The premise is blunt and worth stating plainly, because it's the whole reason this credential exists separately from PAL-E: most organizations make investment, staffing, and prioritization decisions based on the confidence and seniority of whoever's making the argument in the room, not on any actual measurement of the value being delivered or left on the table. EBM is Scrum.org's answer to that — a small set of value areas and measures a leader or coach can use to make the invisible visible, and PAL-EBM tests whether you can actually operate that framework, not just recite its four names.

## Who It's For

PAL-EBM is for leaders, agile coaches, and change agents who need to make an evidence-based case for organizational change to an audience that isn't going to be moved by Scrum theory alone — a CFO, a portfolio committee, a skeptical VP who's heard "agile will make things better" too many times without ever seeing a number attached to it. It's the natural next step for anyone who's already worked through [PAL-E](pal-e.md) and internalized the leadership-behavior side of the picture, and now needs the analytical, evidence-building side to actually move a resourcing or structural decision. It also suits Scrum Masters and agile coaches operating at a portfolio or organizational level — the point in a career where the job stops being "help this one team run good Sprints" and becomes "make the case, with real data, for why three teams should be funded differently than they currently are."

It is not for someone looking for a Scrum Master-facing credential, and it's not a substitute for [Evolving the Agile Organization](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md)'s broader organizational-design and culture material, which PAL-EBM assumes as background rather than re-teaches. Scrum.org doesn't enforce PAL-E as a formal prerequisite, but treating PAL-E's broader leadership-behavior grounding as the honest floor before narrowing into PAL-EBM's specific model is the sane order to take these two in.

## Format & Logistics

- **Format:** No mandatory course required for the assessment itself, consistent with every Scrum.org credential — open-book, timed, multiple-choice/multiple-select, delivered on Scrum.org's own platform with no proctor or testing center. A companion EBM-focused training course exists for those whose employer is funding structured learning, but it's optional.
- **Length and passing score:** Scrum.org has not published PAL-EBM's exact question count, time limit, and passing threshold with the consistency it maintains for PSM I, and this is a narrower, more specialized assessment than the flagship lines. **Verify the current length, time limit, and passing score directly on scrum.org before registering** rather than assuming it mirrors PAL-E's or PSM I's numbers.
- **Cost:** A single flat per-attempt fee paid directly to Scrum.org. **Verify the current price on scrum.org** before budgeting.
- **Retake policy:** Same purchase-another-attempt model as the rest of Scrum.org's catalog, no mandatory waiting period.
- **Prerequisites:** None enforced by Scrum.org, though real familiarity with organizational-level thinking — ideally including PAL-E's material — is the honest floor for this exam to make sense.
- **Validity:** No expiration, no renewal fee, consistent with every Scrum.org Professional-line credential.

## What It Actually Tests

PAL-EBM tests whether you can apply Evidence-Based Management's four key value areas to a real organizational situation — not just define them, but pick the right measure for the right decision and reason about what the evidence is actually telling you. [Evolving the Agile Organization](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md) already introduces the four areas in this guide; PAL-EBM is where that introduction has to become operational fluency:

- **Current Value (CV)** — what the product delivers right now, in terms an organization already tracks: revenue, customer satisfaction, adoption. PAL-EBM scenarios test whether you can select a CV measure that actually reflects the value in question rather than a vanity number that looks good but doesn't move with real customer outcomes.
- **Unrealized Value (UV)** — the gap between what the product delivers today and what it could deliver if it fully met market and customer need. This is the value area leaders most often skip, because it requires admitting how much opportunity is currently on the table — and it's frequently the most persuasive number in a funding conversation, since it reframes "we need more investment" as "here's the size of the opportunity we're currently leaving unaddressed."
- **Time-to-Market (T2M)** — how fast an idea becomes working functionality in customers' hands. This is where EBM connects directly to the flow metrics covered in [Optimizing Flow](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/06-optimizing-flow.md) — cycle time, lead time, release frequency — and PAL-EBM scenarios reward recognizing that a T2M problem is often a flow or hand-off problem hiding behind a "the team is too slow" complaint.
- **Ability to Innovate (A2I)** — how much of the organization's capacity is going toward genuinely new value versus keeping the lights on: defect fixes, manual toil, unmanaged technical debt. A2I is frequently the measure that turns a vague "we're too slow to ship new features" complaint into a specific, fundable case — showing leadership that a majority of Developer capacity is currently consumed by rework, not new capability, is a very different conversation than asking for more headcount in the abstract.

Beyond naming these areas correctly, PAL-EBM tests a sharper, more consequential distinction: using EBM as a genuine decision-support tool versus misusing it as a surveillance or performance-evaluation mechanism. A scenario might describe a leader who starts tracking A2I by team and ranking teams against each other by the result — which looks like "using EBM" but actually violates its purpose, since EBM measures a product or organization's capability, not individual or team performance, and using it punitively destroys the transparency it depends on just as surely as gaming a burndown chart does. The exam also tests whether you understand EBM as a continuous, iterative loop tied to actual experiments and decisions — pick a value area, form a hypothesis, run a small change, measure the effect, adapt — rather than a quarterly scorecard produced once and filed away. That loop is the same empirical pattern the Scrum Guide's inspect-and-adapt cycle runs at the team level, just applied to organizational investment instead of a Sprint Backlog.

## Study Plan

1. **Re-read the Evidence-Based Management section of [Evolving the Agile Organization](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md), slowly, and this time practice generating your own examples for each value area** — not the ones in the text, ones from a product or organization you actually know. PAL-EBM rewards being able to apply the model, not recite its four names.
2. **Go directly to Scrum.org's own Evidence-Based Management Guide, the free whitepaper Scrum.org publishes on the model.** It goes deeper into specific example measures within each value area than any secondhand summary will, and PAL-EBM is written against that material's framing.
3. **Read [Optimizing Flow](../../03-professional-scrum-competencies/06-optimizing-flow.md) with Time-to-Market specifically in mind** — cycle time, lead time, throughput, and WIP limits are the concrete mechanics behind a T2M conversation, and PAL-EBM scenarios expect you to connect flow data to the value-area language leadership actually responds to.
4. **Practice converting vague team complaints into a specific value area and a specific measure.** "The team feels blocked a lot" isn't evidence. "A2I data shows most Developer capacity over the last two quarters went to defect fixes, not new features" is. This translation exercise — turning a feeling into a number a leader can act on — is the actual skill PAL-EBM is checking for.
5. **If you haven't already, work through [PAL-E](pal-e.md) first, or at minimum its material.** PAL-EBM assumes you already understand why leadership behavior matters; it doesn't re-teach that, it just gives you the evidence to argue for changing it.

## Common Failure Points

- **Memorizing the four value areas without being able to pick the right measure for a given scenario.** The exam rarely just asks "what is Current Value" — it gives a situation and asks which value area, and which specific measure within it, actually applies.
- **Misusing EBM as a team- or individual-performance tool in a scenario answer.** Any answer that uses EBM data to rank, punish, or evaluate individual people or teams is a trap — EBM measures organizational and product capability, not personal performance, and using it that way undermines the transparency the whole model depends on.
- **Confusing output metrics with EBM's value-area measures.** Velocity, story points completed, and hours logged are internal team-level outputs, not evidence of value delivered to customers or the organization — a classic distractor is an answer that looks data-driven but is actually just an output metric dressed up as evidence.
- **Treating EBM as a report you generate once rather than a loop tied to real decisions.** A one-time scorecard that nobody acts on isn't Evidence-Based Management in any meaningful sense; the exam rewards recognizing the ongoing hypothesize-measure-adapt cycle over a static snapshot.
- **Skipping Unrealized Value because it's the hardest one to quantify.** UV is deliberately the value area most organizations under-invest in measuring, and PAL-EBM scenarios frequently hinge on recognizing that the opportunity being ignored is exactly what should be surfaced next.
