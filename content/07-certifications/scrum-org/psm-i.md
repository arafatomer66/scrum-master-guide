# PSM I — Professional Scrum Master I

*Scrum.org's entry-level Scrum Master credential, and the one most hiring managers mean when they say "get certified."*

Professional Scrum Master I is the most widely held Scrum Master certification in the industry, and for most developers moving into the role, it's also the right first one. It doesn't test whether you've facilitated a hundred Sprint Reviews or navigated a reorg — it tests whether you understand the Scrum Guide precisely enough to apply it correctly under pressure, including the pressure of a timed exam full of scenarios designed to catch the misconceptions almost everyone starts with. PSM I is Scrum.org's own assessment, not a Scrum Alliance-style "attend a class and get the certificate" credential — nobody hands it to you for showing up. You earn it by passing an online, proctorless, open-book exam, and you can attempt it the same week you decide to.

## Who It's For

PSM I is for developers, testers, analysts, or anyone new to the Scrum Master accountability who wants a credible, self-directed way to prove foundational Scrum knowledge — no employer sponsorship or mandatory course required. It's also common for Product Owners, developers on Scrum Teams, and managers who work alongside Scrum Teams to sit PSM I even when they don't intend to be a Scrum Master, because the exam is really a test of Scrum fluency, not a test of the Scrum Master job specifically. If you're a developer who just got told "you're running the Scrum stuff now," PSM I is the fastest, cheapest way to convert "I've read some blog posts" into a credential a recruiter recognizes.

It is not for someone who already holds PSM I and wants to prove deeper, scenario-heavy judgment — that's [PSM II](psm-ii.md). And it's not a substitute for actually running Scrum on a real team; passing PSM I proves you know the rules, not that you can yet apply them under real organizational friction. Pair it with [section 04, The Scrum Master Role](../../04-scrum-master-role/) before you assume the job itself is covered.

## Format & Logistics

- **Format:** Open-book, untimed-material, but the exam itself is timed. Multiple choice, multiple answer, and true/false questions delivered through Scrum.org's own assessment platform (not a third-party testing center — you take it from your own computer, whenever you want, no appointment or proctor).
- **Length:** Historically 80 questions in 60 minutes, though Scrum.org has periodically adjusted the question count and timing — treat 60 minutes and roughly 80 questions as the right ballpark and **verify the current length and time limit on scrum.org before registering.**
- **Passing score:** Around 85%, which is high compared to most professional certifications. This is intentional — Scrum.org designed PSM I to certify precision, not general familiarity.
- **Cost:** A single flat fee per attempt, paid directly to Scrum.org, in the low hundreds of US dollars. **Verify the current price on scrum.org** — it has changed over the years and varies less by region than Scrum Alliance pricing but is not fixed forever.
- **Retake policy:** If you fail, you can purchase another attempt. Scrum.org does not require a waiting period the way some certification bodies do, though repeated close failures are a strong signal to slow down and study rather than keep re-attempting.
- **Prerequisites:** None. No mandatory training course, no minimum experience, no application review. This is the single biggest structural difference from Scrum Alliance's CSM, which requires a paid, instructor-led course before you're even eligible to sit the exam.
- **Validity:** PSM I does not expire and has no renewal fee or continuing-education requirement — unlike Scrum Alliance credentials, which require periodic renewal (SEUs and a renewal fee). Once you pass, you hold it permanently.

## What It Actually Tests

PSM I tests whether you can apply the Scrum Guide correctly, not whether you can recite it. That distinction matters more than it sounds like it should. A pure recall question would ask "how many Scrum Events are there?" PSM I instead gives you a scenario — a Product Owner wants to add a new item mid-Sprint, a Scrum Master is asked to also manage two other teams' backlogs, a stakeholder shows up uninvited to a Daily Scrum — and asks what Scrum says should happen. You need to know the Guide well enough to reason from it, not just recognize its vocabulary.

The exam draws from across the whole Scrum Guide: the three accountabilities (not "roles" — Scrum.org is precise about this terminology since the 2020 Guide update), the five events and their exact purpose and timebox, the three artifacts and their commitments (Product Goal, Sprint Goal, Definition of Done), the Scrum values, and the empirical pillars of transparency, inspection, and adaptation. It also tests things people assume aren't testable, like exact timeboxes (Sprint Planning is capped at eight hours for a one-month Sprint, scaled down for shorter Sprints) and who has authority over what (only the Developers can change the Sprint Backlog during the Sprint; only the Product Owner can cancel a Sprint, and only if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete).

A meaningful fraction of the questions exist specifically to catch common misconceptions — assumptions almost every new Scrum Master carries in from a traditional project-management background. Recognizing these traps is a bigger lever on your score than any amount of additional Guide trivia:

- **"The Scrum Master assigns tasks to the team."** Always wrong. The Developers self-manage and pull their own work; the Scrum Master has no authority to assign tasks, set deadlines unilaterally, or direct how work gets done.
- **"The Daily Scrum is for the Scrum Master to get a status update."** Always wrong. The Daily Scrum belongs to the Developers, for the Developers, to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and re-plan the next 24 hours. The Scrum Master is not required to attend and never runs it as a status meeting for their own benefit.
- **"The Scrum Master is the team's manager / can override the Developers' decisions."** Always wrong. The Scrum Master has no line authority. They serve the Scrum Team; they don't manage it.
- **"The Product Owner can add items to the Sprint Backlog mid-Sprint whenever they want."** Wrong — only the Developers can change the Sprint Backlog during the Sprint. The Product Owner can renegotiate scope with the Developers, but doesn't unilaterally add to the plan the Developers committed to.
- **"A Sprint can be extended if the team didn't finish."** Wrong. Sprints have a fixed length and are never extended; if work isn't done, it isn't done, and it goes back to the Product Backlog for reprioritization.
- **"Scrum requires estimating in story points."** Wrong. The Scrum Guide does not mandate any specific estimation technique — story points, hours, or no estimation at all are all compatible with Scrum. Questions testing this trap are really testing whether you can distinguish "the Scrum Guide's rules" from "common industry practice layered on top of Scrum," which is a distinction the exam leans on constantly.
- **"The Definition of Done is set by the Scrum Master."** Wrong. It's created by the Developers (or is an organizational standard the Developers must meet), not something the Scrum Master authors and imposes.

If a question's correct answer requires the Scrum Master, Product Owner, or anyone else to grab authority that Scrum explicitly reserves for the Developers, or vice versa, that answer is wrong. This single check resolves a large share of PSM I's trickier questions.

## Study Plan

1. **Read the Scrum Guide itself — more than once.** It's about 13 pages. Most people who fail PSM I on their first attempt did not actually read the current (2020) Scrum Guide carefully; they studied secondhand summaries instead. Read it start to finish at least three times: once for overall shape, once slowly with a highlighter for exact wording (the exam cares about precise language — "the Sprint Backlog is a plan by and for the Developers" is a specific claim, not a paraphrase-friendly one), and once right before the exam as a final refresh. It's free at scrum.org, and because the exam is open-book, being fast at navigating it during the test is itself a skill worth practicing.
2. **Take the free Scrum Open Assessment repeatedly.** Scrum.org publishes this open assessment (30 questions, free, unlimited retakes) as a genuine practice tool, not just a teaser — its question style and difficulty are close to the real exam. Take it, review every wrong answer against the Guide's exact text, retake it a few days later, and keep repeating until you're consistently scoring 95%+ without guessing.
3. **Study the misconception list above until it's automatic.** Most PSM I questions that people miss aren't obscure — they're a variation on "who has authority to do X," and the exam relies on your gut reaction still defaulting to traditional project-management instincts (a manager assigns work, a status meeting reports to a boss, deadlines can flex). Retrain that gut reaction before exam day.
4. **Read [section 02, The Scrum Framework](../../02-scrum-framework/), in this guide** for the same material taught with more real-world framing and worked examples than the Guide's own terse prose provides — useful for the "why" behind the rule you're memorizing, which makes it stick better than rote repetition.
5. **Do a final pass on terminology precision.** Know the difference between an accountability and a role, know that the Scrum Team has three accountabilities (not four, not five), know exact timeboxes for every event, and know which artifact each commitment belongs to (Product Backlog → Product Goal, Sprint Backlog → Sprint Goal, Increment → Definition of Done).

## Common Failure Points

- **Studying a summary instead of the actual Scrum Guide text.** Blog posts and YouTube recaps compress and sometimes subtly misstate the Guide. The exam is written against the Guide's actual wording, and paraphrase-level understanding fails on the sharper questions.
- **Trusting outdated material.** The Scrum Guide was revised in 2020 (removing "roles" in favor of "accountabilities," dropping the "Development Team" as a separate sub-team from the Scrum Team, removing the "servant-leader" framing, among other changes). Pre-2020 study material — including a lot of still-circulating blog content — teaches an older version and will actively mislead you on a handful of questions.
- **Falling for authority-grab distractors.** As covered above: any answer where the Scrum Master directs work, assigns tasks, or overrides the Developers' self-management is wrong on sight. This single heuristic catches a disproportionate share of trap questions.
- **Running out of time because you're using the Guide as a crutch for everything.** Open-book doesn't mean you have time to look up every question. You should know most answers cold and use the Guide only to confirm exact wording or timeboxes on the minority you're unsure of.
- **Overthinking scenario questions by importing outside-Scrum best practices.** PSM I wants "what does Scrum say," not "what would generally be good management practice." A technically reasonable-sounding answer that isn't actually what the Scrum Guide prescribes is still wrong.
