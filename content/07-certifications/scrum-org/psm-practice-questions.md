# PSM Practice Questions

*Fifteen original, scenario-based practice questions in the PSM I/II style — not real exam questions, but written to train the same instincts the real exams test.*

These are written from scratch for this guide, not reproduced or adapted from Scrum.org's actual assessments (which are copyrighted and not publicly reproducible). They mix PSM I-difficulty questions (direct application of a Scrum Guide rule) with PSM II-difficulty questions (ambiguous organizational scenarios where more than one answer sounds reasonable). Each question is followed immediately by the answer and an explanation — cover the answer with your hand if you want a clean attempt first. Where a question has an obvious "trap" distractor, the explanation calls it out by name, because recognizing the trap pattern is worth more than memorizing any single answer.

For the underlying rules these questions draw on, see [section 02, The Scrum Framework](../../02-scrum-framework/). For the judgment-heavy questions, see [section 04, The Scrum Master Role](../../04-scrum-master-role/) and [section 06, Real-Life Scenarios](../../06-real-life-scenarios/).

---

### 1. (PSM I level — Roles)

A stakeholder emails the Scrum Master directly, asking them to make sure two Developers prioritize a specific bug fix today. What should the Scrum Master do?

- A. Assign the bug fix to the two Developers, since the request is time-sensitive.
- B. Tell the stakeholder to raise it with the Product Owner, and make sure the Developers are aware a stakeholder has a concern so they can decide how to handle it.
- C. Add the bug fix to the Sprint Backlog on the stakeholder's behalf.
- D. Refuse to engage, since stakeholders should never contact the Scrum Master.

**Answer: B**

The Scrum Master has no authority to assign work — that's option A's trap, and it's one of the most common misconceptions the exam tests. The Product Owner owns prioritization of what the team works on, and the Developers own how and when they pull work into their day; the Scrum Master's job here is to route the request to the right owner and keep the team informed, not to act as a dispatcher. C is wrong for the same reason as A, just phrased as "adding to the Sprint Backlog" instead of "assigning" — only the Developers change the Sprint Backlog during the Sprint. D is an overcorrection; the Scrum Master should still engage professionally, just not by directing work.

### 2. (PSM I level — Events)

During the Daily Scrum, a Developer starts giving a detailed status update to the Scrum Master, who is standing in for the absent manager. What is the most appropriate response for the Scrum Master?

- A. Take notes on the update to report to the manager later.
- B. Ask the Developer to redirect the update toward the team, focused on progress toward the Sprint Goal and any plan adjustments needed.
- C. Cut the Developer off and cancel the Daily Scrum for the day.
- D. Ask follow-up questions to get more detail, since the Scrum Master needs full visibility into everyone's work.

**Answer: B**

The Daily Scrum is by and for the Developers to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the Sprint Backlog — it is never a status report to the Scrum Master or anyone standing in for a manager. The correct move is to redirect the meeting back to its actual purpose. A and D both reinforce the misconception that the Daily Scrum exists to give someone oversight into individual work, which is the classic trap this question type is built around. C is an overreaction — the fix is redirecting the conversation, not canceling the event.

### 3. (PSM I level — Artifacts)

A Developer says the team's Definition of Done should be relaxed this Sprint because they're behind and want to hit the Sprint Goal. What does Scrum say?

- A. The Definition of Done can be adjusted by the Developers at any time they agree on it.
- B. The Definition of Done cannot be lowered to fit the work — if the work doesn't meet it, it isn't Done and doesn't count as part of the Increment.
- C. The Scrum Master should approve or deny the request.
- D. The Product Owner can override the Definition of Done to protect the Sprint Goal.

**Answer: B**

The Definition of Done is a quality standard, not a negotiable scope lever — lowering it to manufacture the appearance of a finished Sprint Goal is exactly the kind of "adaptation" that's actually a transparency violation. Work that doesn't meet it isn't Done, full stop, regardless of how close the Sprint Goal is. A is the tempting distractor: the Definition of Done can evolve over time by agreement, but not reactively, mid-Sprint, specifically to rescue an unfinished commitment — that's lowering the bar to hide a miss, not genuinely improving quality standards. C and D both hand authority over the Definition of Done to someone other than where it belongs.

### 4. (PSM I level — Events)

A Product Owner wants to skip Sprint Review this Sprint because "there's nothing interesting to show." What's the issue with skipping it?

- A. There's no issue — Sprint Review is optional if there's low-value work to show.
- B. Sprint Review is the primary inspection point for the product with stakeholders, and skipping it removes transparency and a chance to adapt the Product Backlog, regardless of how exciting the Increment feels.
- C. The Scrum Master should hold the Sprint Review alone, without the Developers, to save time.
- D. Sprint Review can be replaced with a written status email instead.

**Answer: B**

Sprint Review is a required inspect-and-adapt event, not an optional showcase gated on how impressive the work looks — its value isn't about putting on a good show, it's about giving stakeholders and the Scrum Team a real chance to inspect the Increment and adapt the Product Backlog together. "Nothing interesting to show" is a judgment call the Product Owner shouldn't be making unilaterally on behalf of transparency itself. C and D both substitute a lower-fidelity, non-collaborative substitute for genuine inspection and adaptation, which defeats the event's purpose even if something technically happens.

### 5. (PSM I level — Scrum Master service)

Who is the Scrum Master accountable to serve, according to the Scrum Guide?

- A. Only the Developers.
- B. Only the Product Owner.
- C. The Scrum Team (Product Owner, Developers, and the Scrum Master's own effectiveness) and the wider organization.
- D. Primarily senior management, since they fund the team.

**Answer: C**

The Scrum Guide is explicit that the Scrum Master serves the Scrum Team in several ways (including the Product Owner and the Developers) and also serves the organization in adopting Scrum. A and B are each true but incomplete — picking either alone misses the full scope of the accountability. D inverts the relationship entirely; the Scrum Master isn't there primarily to serve management's reporting needs.

### 6. (PSM II level — Anti-patterns)

A team has run every Scrum event on schedule for six months, has a clean Sprint Backlog, and never misses a Daily Scrum. Yet the Scrum Master notices the same senior Developer decides what everyone works on every Sprint, and junior Developers rarely speak up in Retrospectives. What is the most accurate read of this situation?

- A. The team is a strong, mature Scrum team since every event happens on schedule.
- B. The team is executing Scrum's mechanics without its underlying self-management principle — the events are present, but decision-making authority hasn't actually moved from an informal hierarchy to the whole team.
- C. The Scrum Master should start assigning tasks more evenly to fix the imbalance directly.
- D. This is normal and expected; every team has an informal leader and that's fine as long as events happen.

**Answer: B**

This is the "mechanics without values" anti-pattern — a team can run every ceremony perfectly and still not be self-managing if authority hasn't genuinely diffused across the team. Doing the events correctly is necessary but not sufficient for Scrum to actually be working. A mistakes surface compliance for maturity, which is the trap. C would have the Scrum Master seize the same kind of unilateral authority they're trying to dissolve, just in the opposite direction — the fix is coaching and facilitation (structured Retrospective techniques that surface quieter voices, working with the senior Developer on sharing decision-making) not directive reassignment. D dismisses a real impediment to self-management as harmless, which under-serves the team's growth.

### 7. (PSM I level — Events)

How long is Sprint Planning timeboxed for a one-month Sprint?

- A. A maximum of four hours.
- B. A maximum of eight hours.
- C. There is no fixed timebox; it ends when the plan feels complete.
- D. Exactly one full working day.

**Answer: B**

Sprint Planning is capped at eight hours for a one-month Sprint, scaled proportionally shorter for shorter Sprints (for example, roughly four hours for a two-week Sprint). This is a precise, memorizable fact the exam does test directly. C is the trap for anyone who's internalized "Scrum avoids rigid rules" too broadly — the events' timeboxes are one of the few genuinely fixed constraints in the framework. D isn't accurate either; a full working day is close to but not the same as an eight-hour cap, and the actual number is worth knowing exactly.

### 8. (PSM II level — Product Ownership / stakeholder dynamics)

A Product Owner consistently says yes to every stakeholder request, resulting in a Product Backlog that changes priority every few days and a team that can never build momentum toward a Sprint Goal. The Scrum Master has raised this with the Product Owner once, without effect. What's the most appropriate next step?

- A. Start filtering stakeholder requests before they reach the Product Owner, to protect the team.
- B. Coach the Product Owner more directly on the cost of constant reprioritization, using concrete evidence from recent Sprints, and involve them in seeing the impact rather than raising it once and stopping.
- C. Escalate to the Product Owner's manager immediately to have them replaced.
- D. Accept it, since Product Backlog ordering is entirely the Product Owner's call and not the Scrum Master's business.

**Answer: B**

Coaching the Product Owner on the impact of their choices — with concrete evidence, persistently, not as a single conversation — is squarely within the Scrum Master's accountability to help the Product Owner understand and practice effective Product Backlog management. A has the Scrum Master quietly taking over Product Owner responsibility instead of coaching the actual owner of the problem, which doesn't build the Product Owner's capability and isn't sustainable. C jumps to an extreme, unilateral escalation before exhausting direct coaching, which is disproportionate this early. D is technically true that ordering is the Product Owner's call, but it ignores that the Scrum Master has a real accountability to help the Product Owner do that job well — "not my business" is the trap for anyone who thinks the Scrum Master's service to the Product Owner is optional.

### 9. (PSM I level — Artifacts)

What is the Sprint Backlog?

- A. A prioritized list of everything the product might ever need.
- B. The plan by and for the Developers, consisting of the Sprint Goal, the selected Product Backlog items, and an actionable plan for delivering the Increment.
- C. A report the Scrum Master keeps to track team velocity.
- D. A document the Product Owner maintains to track what stakeholders were promised.

**Answer: B**

The Sprint Backlog is owned by the Developers, is highly visible, and is a real-time picture of what the Developers plan to accomplish during the Sprint to achieve the Sprint Goal — it's updated throughout the Sprint as more is learned. A describes the Product Backlog, not the Sprint Backlog. C and D each hand ownership of the artifact to someone other than the Developers, which is wrong regardless of how useful either document might sound in practice.

### 10. (PSM II level — Scaling / organizational impediments)

Two Scrum Teams building the same product frequently block each other because a shared component can only be worked on by one team at a time, and neither Product Owner will yield priority to the other. As the Scrum Master for one of the teams, what's the most effective initial move?

- A. Tell your own Developers to avoid that component entirely until the conflict resolves itself.
- B. Make the dependency and its recurring cost visible to both Product Owners and both Scrum Masters together, and facilitate a conversation about resolving the structural conflict, rather than treating it as a one-team problem.
- C. Escalate directly to senior management to force a decision, without first involving the other team's Scrum Master or Product Owner.
- D. Accept the blocking as a normal cost of working with another team and stop raising it.
- E. Merge the two Sprint Backlogs into one shared backlog so there's no ambiguity about priority.

**Answer: B**

A cross-team dependency conflict is a systemic, organizational-level impediment, and the Scrum Master's accountability extends to helping the organization see and remove impediments beyond their own team — which starts with making the pattern visible to the people who can actually resolve it together, not solving it unilaterally or alone. A quietly avoids the real problem instead of surfacing it. C skips direct collaboration with peers in favor of an escalation that a persistent, unaddressed impediment might eventually warrant, but not as the first move. D normalizes a genuinely fixable structural problem. E conflates two teams' Sprint Backlogs, which blurs each team's own plan and ownership rather than fixing the underlying resource contention — the actual fix is more likely to be addressing the shared-component structure itself (a Nexus-style integration approach, a shared component team, or a genuine reprioritization conversation), not merging artifacts.

### 11. (PSM I level — Roles / accountabilities)

Who is accountable for the overall value of the product delivered by the Scrum Team?

- A. The Scrum Master.
- B. The Product Owner.
- C. The Developers.
- D. The most senior Developer.

**Answer: B**

The Product Owner is accountable for maximizing the value of the product resulting from the work of the Scrum Team. The Scrum Master serves the team and the organization but doesn't own product value directly (A). The Developers are accountable for creating a valuable, useful Increment every Sprint and for the quality of their work, but the accountability for overall product value sits with the Product Owner (C). D isn't a Scrum accountability at all — Scrum doesn't formally recognize an internal hierarchy among Developers.

### 12. (PSM II level — Facilitation / coaching stance)

A newly formed team keeps asking the Scrum Master to resolve every disagreement during Sprint Planning, even small ones, because "that's what Scrum Masters are for." What's the best long-term response?

- A. Keep resolving the disagreements directly, since it keeps Sprint Planning moving efficiently.
- B. Refuse to help at all, since the Developers should be fully self-managing from day one.
- C. Facilitate the team through resolving disagreements themselves, teaching the underlying facilitation technique so they need less help over time — accepting some short-term slowness as an investment in the team's growth.
- D. Ask the Product Owner to make all Sprint Planning decisions instead, to remove ambiguity.

**Answer: C**

This is a coaching-stance question: the right response depends on meeting the team where they are and building capability, not defaulting to either extreme. A trades long-term self-management for short-term speed and keeps the team dependent on the Scrum Master indefinitely — a common trap because it looks helpful. B ignores that self-management is a skill teams grow into, not a default state a new team already has; refusing help outright isn't coaching, it's abandonment. D hands Sprint Planning decision authority to the Product Owner, which isn't where it belongs — Sprint Planning is a collaborative event, and the plan is by and for the Developers.

### 13. (PSM I level — Values / empiricism)

A team's Sprint Burndown chart shows the team is "behind" halfway through the Sprint. A manager asks the Scrum Master to have the team work overtime to get back on track. What Scrum principle is most directly relevant to how the Scrum Master should respond?

- A. Predictability — the team committed to the forecast and must honor it exactly.
- B. Transparency and empiricism — a burndown is a forecast based on incomplete information, and the team's response should come from their own inspection and adaptation, not from a manager imposing a fix based on a mid-Sprint snapshot.
- C. The Scrum Master should approve the overtime request to protect the Sprint Goal.
- D. Courage — the team should push through no matter the cost, since Courage is a Scrum value.
- E. None of the values apply here since burndown charts aren't part of the Scrum Guide.

**Answer: B**

A mid-Sprint burndown is a data point for inspection, not a contract — the correct response is for the Developers to inspect their own progress and adapt their plan (which might mean re-scoping, not overtime), grounded in transparency and empiricism rather than a manager's reaction to an incomplete chart. A treats a forecast as a fixed commitment, which misunderstands what burndown data is for. C has the Scrum Master approving a decision that isn't theirs to approve — sustainable pace and workload decisions belong to the Developers. D misuses the Scrum value of Courage to justify unsustainable overtime, which is the opposite of what Courage means in the Scrum Guide (having the courage to do the right thing and work on tough problems — not to absorb bad management pressure silently). E is wrong because even though burndown charts specifically aren't mandated by the Scrum Guide, the underlying principles of transparency and empiricism absolutely apply to how the team uses whatever forecasting tool it has.

### 14. (PSM II level — Retrospective / continuous improvement anti-pattern)

A team's Sprint Retrospectives consistently produce the same three action items Sprint after Sprint, and none of them are ever actually completed. What's the most likely underlying issue, and what should the Scrum Master do?

- A. The team is bad at Retrospectives and the Scrum Master should start writing the action items for them.
- B. The action items are probably too broad, not owned by anyone specific, and not tracked anywhere visible between Retrospectives — the Scrum Master should help the team make improvement items small, owned, and tracked with the same visibility as Sprint Backlog work.
- C. Retrospectives should be held less often, since they're clearly not producing value.
- D. This is normal; not every improvement idea needs to be acted on.

**Answer: B**

Recurring, never-completed action items are a well-known Retrospective anti-pattern, and the fix is almost always structural: items that are too vague or too large never get prioritized against Sprint work and quietly die. The Scrum Master's job is to facilitate better Retrospective outcomes — concrete, owned, small, and visible — not to do the follow-through for the team (A) or to conclude the event itself has no value and cut it (C). D rationalizes a real, fixable pattern as acceptable, which misses a genuine coaching opportunity; a Retrospective that never produces followed-through improvement isn't fulfilling its purpose.

### 15. (PSM I level — Events / Sprint integrity)

Partway through a Sprint, the market shifts and the Sprint Goal becomes irrelevant. Who has the authority to cancel the Sprint?

- A. The Scrum Master, since protecting the team from wasted work is part of their service.
- B. The Product Owner, and only the Product Owner — and only when the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete.
- C. The Developers, by majority vote.
- D. Senior management, since they're ultimately responsible for the product's success.

**Answer: B**

The Scrum Guide reserves Sprint cancellation authority specifically and exclusively for the Product Owner, and only when the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete — it's one of the more precisely scoped rules in the Guide and a favorite target for exam questions precisely because every other option sounds plausible. A is tempting because it frames the action as "protecting the team," which is a genuine part of the Scrum Master's service, but authority to cancel the Sprint still isn't theirs. C and D both hand the decision to parties the Scrum Guide doesn't authorize for this specific action, however reasonable their involvement might feel in a real organization.
