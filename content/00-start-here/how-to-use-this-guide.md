# How to Use This Guide

*Orientation for developers landing here for the first time — what this repo is, who it's for, and three ways to read it depending on where you're starting from.*

If someone on your team just said "can you be our Scrum Master for a bit" and you nodded and then went and searched the internet, you're in the right place. This guide exists because most Scrum content sits at one of two extremes: the Scrum Guide itself, which is 13 pages of deliberately minimal rules that tell you *what* to do but almost nothing about *how*, or a training vendor's landing page trying to sell you a two-day course. The space between those two — what a Scrum Master actually does on a Tuesday when the Sprint is falling apart, the Product Owner is on leave, and two engineers are quietly refusing to talk to each other — is where this guide lives.

## Who this is for

This is written for developers, not career agile consultants. That means a few things about how it's pitched:

- It assumes you already know how to reason about systems, read a process critically, and spot when something is theater versus substance — skills any competent engineer already has.
- It does not assume you've ever run a meeting, coached a person through a conflict, or read a page of organizational psychology. Those skills get built from zero, explicitly, not assumed.
- It is written for three overlapping audiences: developers or PMs who've been asked to facilitate Scrum events without the title, engineers who've taken (or are about to take) an actual Scrum Master role, and anyone studying for a certification exam (PSM, CSM, or SAFe) who wants material with more depth than an exam-cram sheet.

If you want the longer version of what a realistic path through this role looks like — including how long each stage actually takes and what tends to go wrong — read [The Scrum Master Career Path](scrum-master-career-path.md) next.

## What's actually in here

The repo is organized as ten numbered sections, `00` through `09`, and the numbering is the intended reading order for someone starting from zero — though almost nobody should read it strictly top to bottom (more on that below).

- **00 · Start Here** — this section. Orientation and the career roadmap.
- **01 · Agile Foundations** — why Scrum exists at all: the 2001 Agile Manifesto explained line by line, Scrum versus Waterfall without strawmanning either, and the empirical process control / lean thinking theory that everything else in Scrum is built on top of.
- **02 · The Scrum Framework** — the 2020 Scrum Guide itself, unpacked: the three pillars, the accountabilities (Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers), the five events, the three artifacts, and the five values.
- **03 · Professional Scrum Competencies** — an original synthesis mapped to Scrum.org's six-area competency model (Developing People and Teams, Facilitating Collaboration, Managing Products with Agility, Developing and Delivering Products Professionally, Evolving the Agile Organization, Understanding Organizational Design). This is the closest thing to a shared syllabus across every certification body, so it's worth reading regardless of which cert you're aiming for.
- **04 · The Scrum Master Role** — the job itself, in practice: facilitation technique, coaching versus mentoring versus teaching, useful and misleading metrics, conflict handling, and a catalog of anti-patterns (the "Scrum Master as secretary" trap, the "Scrum Master as project manager in disguise" trap, and others).
- **05 · Scaling Frameworks** — Nexus, Scrum@Scale, LeSS, and SAFe, covering when scaling is actually the right call versus when it's organizational overhead dressed up as rigor.
- **06 · Real-Life Scenarios** — twenty situations worked end to end: the situation, why it happens, the Scrum Master's approach step by step, the likely outcome, and how a certification exam would frame the same situation as a question. This is the most concretely useful section in the guide if you already know the framework and want to see it applied.
- **07 · Certifications** — full study guides for PSM I/II/III (Scrum.org), CSM/A-CSM/CSP-SM (Scrum Alliance), and SAFe Scrum Master/Advanced Scrum Master (Scaled Agile). Not sure which one applies to you? Start with [Which Certification Should I Get](../07-certifications/00-which-certification-should-i-get.md).
- **08 · Templates and Toolkit** — ready-to-use ceremony agendas, retrospective formats, and checklists you can copy into your own team's tooling today.
- **09 · Interview Prep** — a question bank for Scrum Master job interviews, since "I read the Scrum Guide" is not an answer that survives a real interview panel.

## Three ways to read this, depending on where you're starting

**You're brand new to Agile.** Start at `01 · Agile Foundations`, then move to `02 · The Scrum Framework`. Don't skip 01 even if you're impatient to get to "the actual job" — the reason Scrum has a Sprint, a Daily Scrum, and a Definition of Done all traces back to empirical process control, and if you don't have that theory, every practice in `02` looks like arbitrary ceremony instead of a deliberate answer to a specific problem. From there, move to `03` for the competency model and `04` for the job itself.

**You're already doing the job — with or without the title.** You likely don't need `01` or `02` as a first read (skim them for gaps, don't start there). Go straight to `04 · The Scrum Master Role` and then read every case in `06 · Real-Life Scenarios`. That's where the job actually lives: not in knowing what a Sprint Retrospective is, but in knowing what to do when the same two people dominate every retrospective and the rest of the team has checked out.

**You're studying for a certification.** Read `03 · Professional Scrum Competencies` first, regardless of which certification body you're sitting with — PSM, CSM, and SAFe all draw from the same underlying competency set even though they test it differently. Then go straight to your certification's page under `07`, which links back into `03` and `06` at the specific points the exam tends to probe. PSM and CSM exams in particular are scenario-based, not recall-based, which is exactly why `06` exists as its own section instead of being folded into `04`.

## What this is not

This is not a marketing page for a certification body, a consultancy, or a specific framework. Nobody selling SAFe training wrote the SAFe section, and nobody with a PSM affiliate link wrote the PSM section — there is no affiliate link anywhere in this repo. That matters because a lot of what's published about Scrum Mastery online is written to get you to buy a course, which creates an incentive to inflate the value of the credential and understate how much of the job is unglamorous, situational judgment that no course teaches.

It's also not a substitute for doing the job badly a few times and learning from it. No amount of reading replaces the first time you run a retrospective and nobody says anything for four straight minutes, or the first time you have to tell a Product Owner their pet feature isn't making the Sprint. What this guide can do is make sure you're not learning the fundamentals — what empiricism actually buys you, why the events exist, what "servant leadership" means beyond the buzzword — the hard way, at the same time you're learning everything else.

Read [The Scrum Master Career Path](scrum-master-career-path.md) next to see the realistic multi-year arc this all sits inside, or jump straight to [The Agile Manifesto](../01-agile-foundations/agile-manifesto.md) if you want to start from the theoretical ground floor.
