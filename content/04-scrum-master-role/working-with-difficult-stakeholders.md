# Working with Difficult Stakeholders

*Three recurring stakeholder dysfunctions a Scrum Master will face repeatedly, and specific, sayable scripts for handling each without becoming either a pushover or a wall.*

Most "difficult stakeholder" problems aren't actually about difficult people — they're predictable patterns that show up on nearly every team, and they respond well to a prepared, consistent approach rather than improvising fresh each time. Here are three of the most common, with what a Scrum Master should actually say and do.

## The stakeholder who wants mid-sprint scope changes

This is the single most frequent stakeholder friction point, and it usually comes from a reasonable place — a genuinely urgent request, a customer escalation, a boss asking for a favor — which is exactly why it's hard to just say no.

**What the Scrum Master does:** Never personally approve or reject the change — that's not the Scrum Master's call to make, and making it yourself puts you between the Product Owner and the stakeholder in a way that undermines the PO's actual accountability. Redirect immediately and consistently to the Product Owner, and make the tradeoff visible rather than invisible.

**What the Scrum Master says:** "That sounds important — let's make sure it gets prioritized properly. Right now we're mid-sprint on a committed Sprint Goal, so adding this means either it waits until the next Sprint Planning, or [Product Owner] decides something else comes out to make room. I can't make that call, but let's go find them right now so it doesn't sit in limbo." This does three things in one breath: it doesn't dismiss the stakeholder, it makes the cost of the change explicit instead of pretending it's free, and it routes the actual decision to the person accountable for it.

If this is a recurring stakeholder, not a one-off, it's worth a separate conversation with the Product Owner about a standing policy — e.g., an agreed "emergency lane" for genuinely urgent items with a pre-defined cost (something specific always gets pulled to make room) rather than relitigating the tradeoff from scratch every time. See [Stakeholder Demands Mid-Sprint Scope Change](../06-real-life-scenarios/04-stakeholder-demands-mid-sprint-scope-change.md) for a full worked scenario, and [Shielding vs. Enabling](shielding-vs-enabling.md) for when it's right for the Scrum Master to keep handling this personally versus coaching the Product Owner and team to hold this line themselves.

## The executive who wants a hard date on a complex, uncertain item

This one usually isn't malicious either — executives are frequently the ones who have to commit to something in front of a board, a customer, or a budget cycle, and "we don't know yet" is a genuinely uncomfortable answer for them to carry upward. But agreeing to a fabricated date to make the conversation easier is worse than the discomfort of not having one.

**What the Scrum Master does:** Don't argue about whether a date is knowable in the abstract — show the actual data and offer something more useful than a single date: a range with a confidence level, plus a concrete way to reduce the uncertainty.

**What the Scrum Master says:** "I understand you need something you can plan around, and I want to give you something you can actually trust rather than a number that sounds precise but isn't real. Based on our last six sprints, here's our throughput range — at that rate, we're 85% confident this is done by [date], and 50% confident by [earlier date]. If a hard commitment is required sooner than that, the fastest way to tighten that range is a two-week discovery spike to cut the biggest unknowns — after that we'll have a much tighter number." This gives the executive something concrete to act on (a range, a confidence level, a path to more certainty) instead of either a stonewalled "we can't say" or a fabricated date that quietly becomes a promise nobody can keep. See [Metrics and Forecasting](metrics-and-forecasting.md) for exactly how that range and confidence level get built from real throughput data rather than being invented on the spot.

If the executive pushes past this and insists on a single hard date regardless, that's the moment to be explicit about what's being traded away: "I can give you a date if that's what's needed, but I want to be honest that it'll be a guess dressed up as a commitment, and if we miss it, that's why. I'd rather you go in with the range." Sometimes the executive still wants the single date for external reasons the Scrum Master doesn't control — that's a legitimate organizational reality, but the Scrum Master's job is to make sure everyone in the room knows exactly what kind of number they're looking at.

## The stakeholder who skips Sprint Review then complains about the outcome

This pattern erodes trust in the Sprint Review as an institution, because it teaches the team that showing up to demo the Increment doesn't actually matter — the same complaints arrive either way, just later and with more frustration attached.

**What the Scrum Master does:** Address it on two timescales — proactively, before the pattern repeats, and directly, after it happens again.

**Proactively**, make the Review genuinely worth attending rather than assuming attendance is the stakeholder's problem to solve: send a short, specific note beforehand naming exactly what will be shown and why it matters to that stakeholder ("we'll be demoing the new checkout flow — this directly affects the conversion issue you raised last month"), not a generic calendar invite. **After the Review**, send a short written or recorded summary to anyone who missed it, so the option to give feedback still technically exists, even though it's a worse channel than being in the room.

**What the Scrum Master says**, directly and without hostility, when the complaint arrives after the fact: "I hear the concern, and I want to make sure it gets addressed properly — the Sprint Review is exactly the forum built for this, since the team can respond and adjust in real time instead of it landing after the fact when the sprint's already closed. I'd really like you there next time; is there something about the timing or format that's making it hard to attend, that we could change?" This does two things: it doesn't just absorb the complaint and quietly route it into the backlog as if nothing's wrong with the pattern, and it treats the non-attendance as a solvable logistics problem first, rather than assuming indifference.

If the stakeholder genuinely can't attend for a structural reason (time zone, competing recurring commitment), it's worth working with the Product Owner on an alternative — a shorter recorded walkthrough sent the same day, or a standing async channel for Review feedback — rather than letting "they didn't come" become the permanent, unaddressed excuse for late, unstructured complaints.

## The common thread

All three scripts share the same shape: acknowledge the stakeholder's real concern without immediately capitulating to it, make the actual tradeoff or cost visible instead of absorbing it silently, and route the decision to whoever actually holds the authority to make it — usually the Product Owner, sometimes the team, rarely the Scrum Master personally. A Scrum Master who either caves every time or stonewalls every time trains stakeholders to either walk over the team or avoid it entirely; consistency in how these conversations go is what eventually produces stakeholders who bring requests through the right channel without needing to be redirected every single time.
