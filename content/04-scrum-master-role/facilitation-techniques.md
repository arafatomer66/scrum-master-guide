# Facilitation Techniques

*The concrete mechanics of running a productive meeting without steering the outcome: diverge/converge, dot-voting, silent brainstorming, timeboxing, parking lots, and handling dominant voices or silence.*

Facilitation is the most learnable skill in the Scrum Master toolkit, and also the one most Scrum Masters never formally learn — they pick up bad habits from bad meetings and repeat them. Good facilitation is a set of specific techniques, not a personality trait. Here are the ones that earn their place in every ceremony, from [Sprint Retrospective](../02-scrum-framework/sprint-retrospective.md) to Backlog Refinement to an ad-hoc problem-solving session.

## Diverge before you converge

Most unproductive meetings fail because the group tries to generate ideas and evaluate them at the same time. Someone proposes an option, someone else immediately critiques it, and the group narrows prematurely around whatever survived the first round of scrutiny — usually the safest, least interesting idea.

The fix is structural: separate divergence (generate as many options as possible, no judgment) from convergence (narrow down to what you'll actually act on) into two explicit phases, and say so out loud. "For the next five minutes we're only generating ideas — no discussion, no 'but,' just get it out. We'll evaluate everything after." This single sentence, said at the start, changes the room's behavior more than almost any other facilitation move.

Convergence then has its own techniques — affinity grouping (cluster similar ideas together on the board before discussing them as clusters rather than individually) and dot-voting.

## Dot-voting

Give each participant a fixed number of votes — typically three — and let them place a mark (a literal dot sticker on a physical board, or a click/emoji in a tool like Miro or Mural) next to the options they most want the group to focus on. People can put all three dots on one item if they feel strongly, or spread them across three.

This does two things a verbal discussion can't: it surfaces the group's actual weighting quickly (five minutes instead of twenty of debate), and it gives quieter participants equal voice — their vote counts the same as the most vocal person's, which isn't true of an open-floor discussion. After dot-voting, don't just take the top vote-getter and move on; ask "does anyone feel strongly that a low-vote item shouldn't be dropped?" before you finalize, since dot-voting can accidentally bury a critical minority concern.

## Silent brainstorming before group discussion

If you open a retrospective or a problem-solving session by asking "so, thoughts?", you will reliably get the same one or two people talking first, and everyone else's thinking gets anchored to whatever framing that first person set — even people who privately disagree. This is anchoring bias, and it's one of the most consistent failure modes in group facilitation.

The fix: silent, individual brainstorming first. Give everyone three to five minutes to write their own thoughts on sticky notes (physical or digital) before anyone speaks. Only after that individual thinking is captured do you open the floor, and even then, read the notes aloud yourself or have people place them on the board before discussing — don't let the first spoken opinion set the frame. This one technique does more to surface genuine minority views and introvert input than any amount of "make sure everyone gets a turn" goodwill.

## Timeboxing

A timebox isn't a suggestion — it's a hard container that forces prioritization. Two habits make timeboxing actually work rather than becoming theater:

- **Make the timer visible to everyone**, not just to you. A shared screen countdown or a physical timer in the room means the group self-regulates instead of relying on you to be the bad guy who cuts people off.
- **Call the two-minute warning out loud** before time runs out — "two minutes left on this topic" — so the group can converge naturally instead of getting cut off mid-thought. When time actually expires and the topic isn't resolved, the default move is the parking lot, not silently letting the timebox slide.

Sprint Planning, the Daily Scrum, and the Sprint Retrospective all have Guide-recommended maximum durations tied to sprint length — treat those as real constraints you protect, not aspirational targets. See [Daily Scrum](../02-scrum-framework/daily-scrum.md) for what happens when that discipline erodes.

## The parking lot

Any topic that's important but off-topic for the current conversation goes on a visibly maintained "parking lot" — a physical flip-chart sheet or a column in your digital board — rather than being either derailed into or steamrolled out of the conversation. Two disciplines make this work: write the item down where everyone can see it happen (so the person raising it feels heard, not shut down), and actually revisit the parking lot before the meeting ends or explicitly schedule when it will be addressed. A parking lot that's never revisited teaches the team that raising things is pointless, which kills future participation.

## Facilitating without injecting your own opinion

The hardest discipline for a Scrum Master who has domain opinions (and most do) is staying neutral on content while actively shaping process. Concrete techniques:

- Ask, don't assert: "what would happen if we tried X?" instead of "we should do X."
- Reflect back rather than respond: "so what I'm hearing is two different views — one says A, one says B. Is that right?" This makes disagreement visible and owned by the group, not filtered through you.
- If you do have a strong opinion the team genuinely needs to hear, name the mode switch explicitly: "I'm going to step out of facilitator mode for a second and give you my actual opinion as a team member — then I'll go back to facilitating." That's a legitimate move, but it only stays honest if you flag it every time, not just when convenient.

## Handling a dominant voice

Left unmanaged, one confident, fast-talking person will fill 80% of a meeting's airtime without anyone deciding that should happen. Direct techniques:

- **Round-robin**: go person by person, in order, so airtime is structurally distributed rather than first-come-first-served.
- **"Let's hear from folks who haven't spoken yet"** — a direct, non-confrontational redirect that doesn't single anyone out by name.
- **Written input first** (see silent brainstorming above) — this neutralizes the dominant-voice problem before it starts, because everyone's initial thinking is captured before anyone speaks.
- If the pattern persists across multiple meetings, address it privately, not in the room: "I've noticed you tend to jump in first and it sometimes shortens the discussion before others weigh in — would you be open to holding back for the first couple minutes next time?" This is a coaching conversation, not a public correction. See [Coaching vs. Mentoring vs. Teaching](coaching-vs-mentoring-vs-teaching.md).

## Handling silence

New facilitators treat silence as a failure and rush to fill it — often by answering their own question. Resist this. After asking a real question, silently count to ten before saying anything else. Most people need a few seconds to actually think, especially about a question that requires reflection rather than recall, and the most valuable comment in a retrospective often comes from someone who needed that extra beat. If silence persists past ten or fifteen seconds, don't answer for the group — rephrase the question, or fall back to silent writing so people who think better on paper than out loud have a path in.

For the specific case where silence or a dominant voice is a symptom of unresolved tension rather than just a group dynamics issue, see [Conflict Resolution](conflict-resolution.md).
