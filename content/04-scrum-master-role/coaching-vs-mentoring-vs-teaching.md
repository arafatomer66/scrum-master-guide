# Coaching vs. Mentoring vs. Teaching

*Three distinct modes a Scrum Master moves between constantly — coaching unlocks someone's own answer through questions, mentoring shares your own experience, teaching transfers a specific skill — and using the wrong one is a common, fixable mistake.*

New Scrum Masters, and plenty of experienced ones, default to a single mode for everything: give advice. Someone raises a problem, and the instinct is to solve it for them. That's sometimes exactly right, and often exactly wrong. The three modes below aren't interchangeable, and picking the wrong one for the situation either wastes time (teaching someone who already knows the answer and just needed to be heard) or leaves someone stranded (coaching someone who genuinely doesn't have the knowledge yet and needs to be told).

## Coaching: unlocking someone's own answer

Coaching assumes the person already has the capability to solve their own problem — what's missing isn't information, it's clarity, and questions get them there faster than advice does. The coach doesn't know the "right" answer and isn't trying to steer toward one; they're helping the other person think out loud in a structured way. A common structure is GROW — **Goal** (what do you want), **Reality** (what's actually happening now), **Options** (what could you do), **Will** (what will you actually commit to).

**Example dialogue** — a developer complaining that a teammate keeps rewriting their code in code review:

> Developer: "He keeps rewriting my PRs instead of just commenting. It's demoralizing."
> Scrum Master: "What outcome would you actually want from code review?"
> Developer: "I guess... feedback I can act on myself, not just have someone else do it for me."
> Scrum Master: "Have you told him that directly?"
> Developer: "No, I've just been quietly annoyed."
> Scrum Master: "What's one option for saying that to him this week?"

Notice the Scrum Master never says what the developer should do. The developer arrives at "I need to talk to him directly" themselves, which means they're far more likely to actually do it — and they've practiced a skill (naming the issue) they'll need again.

## Mentoring: sharing your own experience

Mentoring is different: it explicitly draws on the mentor's own lived experience, offered as one input among several the other person can use or discard. It's appropriate when the person is asking, implicitly or explicitly, "what would you do" — and when the Scrum Master genuinely has relevant experience to offer, not just an opinion dressed up as one.

**Example dialogue** — a newer Scrum Master asking how to handle a Product Owner who keeps changing priorities mid-sprint:

> New SM: "Every sprint the PO wants to swap something in halfway through. How do I stop that?"
> Mentor: "I had almost this exact situation on a team I worked with a couple years back. What worked for me was making the cost visible, not just saying no — I'd literally show her the burndown and ask 'what do you want to pull out to make room for this.' It didn't stop her from asking, but it made every ask a real tradeoff instead of a free add. That won't necessarily work the same way for you, but it might be worth trying."

The mentor is transparent that this is *their* experience, not a universal rule, and leaves room for the new Scrum Master to adapt or reject it. That's the line between mentoring and just handing down orders.

## Teaching: transferring a specific skill or fact

Teaching is the most direct mode: there's a concrete piece of knowledge or a technique the other person doesn't yet have, and the fastest path is simply to give it to them. No amount of coaching questions will help someone who has literally never seen dot-voting figure out how dot-voting works — you just show them.

**Example dialogue** — a team about to run its first retrospective:

> Developer: "How is this different from just talking about what went wrong?"
> Scrum Master: "We're going to do it in three steps. First, everyone silently writes what went well, what didn't, and what to try next — on separate sticky notes, one idea per note, five minutes, no talking. Then we'll group similar notes together on the board. Then we'll dot-vote on which cluster to actually spend time discussing, because we won't have time to discuss everything. Let's start the timer."

There's no ambiguity here, no unlocking required — it's instruction. Teaching mode is also correct for things like explaining what a Definition of Done is, how the sprint burndown is calculated, or what a specific Scrum Guide term means. See [Facilitation Techniques](facilitation-techniques.md) for more of the specific mechanics a Scrum Master frequently has to teach.

## Choosing the right mode

A rough rule of thumb: **teach** when the gap is knowledge of a mechanic or fact ("what is a spike," "how does dot-voting work"); **mentor** when someone is facing a judgment call and explicitly wants to know how someone more experienced has handled it; **coach** as the default for anything involving the team's own ownership, especially interpersonal friction, self-management, or decisions the team needs to actually believe in rather than just execute. Coaching should be the most-used mode for a Scrum Master specifically because the accountability described across [Servant Leadership](servant-leadership.md) and the Guide's [service areas](the-4-sm-service-areas.md) centers on growing the team's own capability, not on the Scrum Master having all the answers.

A tell that you're in the wrong mode: if you catch yourself giving advice nobody asked for, pause and ask a question instead — you may have jumped straight to mentoring or teaching when the person actually needed to think it through themselves. Conversely, if you're asking a brand-new team member open-ended coaching questions about something they've genuinely never encountered before (say, what a Sprint Review is for), you're withholding information they need, not respecting their autonomy.

This distinction also shapes how a Scrum Master should run one-on-ones — see the [One-on-One Question Bank](../08-templates-and-toolkit/one-on-one-question-bank.md) for coaching-mode questions organized by situation, and [Developing People and Teams](../03-professional-scrum-competencies/02-developing-people-and-teams.md) for how this fits the broader competency of growing a team's capability over time.
