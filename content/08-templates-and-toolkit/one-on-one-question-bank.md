# 1:1 Question Bank for Scrum Masters

*Twenty-eight real questions for one-on-one conversations with team members, organized by purpose, with guidance on asking instead of telling.*

A Scrum Master's 1:1 is not a status meeting and, in most organizations, not a performance conversation either — that's a line manager's job, and conflating the two is one of the fastest ways to destroy the psychological safety a Scrum Master depends on. If your organization does have you holding some formal people-management responsibility, keep that conversation explicitly separate from this one, even if it's the same person in the room on a different day. The purpose of a Scrum Master 1:1 is closer to a coach's check-in: understand how this person is actually doing, surface what they wouldn't say in a group setting, and default to questions over advice — see [Coaching vs. Mentoring vs. Teaching](../04-scrum-master-role/coaching-vs-mentoring-vs-teaching.md) for when each stance is actually the right one.

**Cadence.** Every 2-4 weeks per person is typical for a Scrum Master (more often than that and it starts crowding out the person's actual work; less often and problems go stale before you hear about them). 20-30 minutes is usually enough. Keep it on the calendar even in a good Sprint — the value compounds from consistency, not from only showing up when something's wrong.

**Ground rules worth stating out loud the first time:** this isn't a status update (the Daily Scrum and board cover that); nothing said here becomes ammunition in a performance review; you'll ask before repeating anything specific to anyone else, including their manager.

## Checking in on wellbeing and burnout

1. "How are you doing, really — not the Daily Scrum version of the answer?"
2. "On a scale from energized to running on empty, where are you this week, and what's driving that?"
3. "Is there anything outside work making this a harder stretch than usual? You don't need to share details — I just want to know if I should expect less bandwidth from you and plan around it."
4. "When's the last time you left work feeling genuinely good about something you built?"
5. "Does the pace of the last few Sprints feel sustainable, or are we borrowing against next month to pay for this one?"
6. "What's one thing that would make next week noticeably less stressful for you?"
7. "Are you actually stepping away — lunch, breaks, end of day — or has it been heads-down the whole time lately?"

## Surfacing hidden impediments

8. "What slowed you down this week that never came up in the Daily Scrum?"
9. "Is there anything you're waiting on — from me, from another team, from a decision that hasn't been made — that's been stuck longer than it should be?"
10. "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one recurring annoyance about how we work, what would it be?"
11. "Is there a tool, access, or piece of information you need that you don't currently have?"
12. "Who do you find yourself waiting on most often, and why do you think that keeps happening?"
13. "Is there a decision sitting unmade somewhere that's quietly costing us time?"
14. "What's something you'd never bring up in front of the whole team but would tell me one-on-one?"

## Coaching-style open questions (vs. advice-giving)

The instinct when someone brings a problem is to solve it for them. That's often faster in the moment and worse for the team over time — it trains people to bring you problems instead of building their own judgment. Default to the right column; reach for the left only when the person is genuinely stuck or the stakes and time pressure don't allow for coaching.

| Instead of telling them | Try asking |
|---|---|
| "You should talk to [person] directly." | "What's stopping you from raising that with [person] yourself?" |
| "Here's how I'd solve that." | "What have you already tried?" |
| "That's not a big deal, don't worry about it." | "What would 'good' look like to you here?" |
| "Just say no to the extra scope." | "What's the smallest next step you could take, and when will you take it?" |

15. "What have you already tried?"
16. "What do you think is really going on here, underneath the surface issue?"
17. "What would 'good' look like to you in this situation?"
18. "What's holding you back from doing that?"
19. "If I weren't in the room, what would you actually do?"
20. "What's the smallest next step you could take, and when will you take it?"
21. "What's stopping you from raising that with [teammate] directly instead of routing it through me?"

## Career growth conversations

22. "Where do you want to be a year from now — skill-wise, scope-wise, role-wise?"
23. "What's something you did recently that you'd like to do a lot more of?"
24. "Is there a skill that feels like it's going stale because the current work doesn't stretch it?"
25. "Whose role or whose work here looks interesting to you, and what about it appeals to you?"
26. "Is there feedback you've been sitting on that you haven't given anyone yet — about the team, about me, about how we work?"
27. "If you could pair with or shadow anyone on a piece of work right now, who would it be and why?"
28. "What would make you excited to come to work that isn't currently part of your day-to-day?"

## Using this bank well

Don't run through a category checklist mechanically — pick one or two questions that fit what's actually going on with that person that week. A question from the wellbeing section after a visibly rough Sprint lands very differently than the same question asked cold in a routine check-in. If a question surfaces something real — a burnout signal, an impediment that's been silently costing the team time, a career interest that points toward a change worth making — treat that as the actual outcome of the 1:1, not a tangent from it. Follow up on what you heard at the next 1:1 by name ("last time you mentioned X — how's that sitting now?"); a Scrum Master who asks good questions once and never circles back trains people to stop answering honestly.
