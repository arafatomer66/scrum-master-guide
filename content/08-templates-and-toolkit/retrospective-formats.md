# Retrospective Formats

*Eleven real Sprint Retrospective formats with a one-paragraph how-to each, guidance on rotating them to avoid retro fatigue, and a template for making sure every retro ends in owned, trackable action items.*

A [Sprint Retrospective](../02-scrum-framework/sprint-retrospective.md) is the one Scrum event with almost no prescribed structure — the Guide says the team inspects how the last Sprint went in terms of people, relationships, process, and tools, identifies improvements, and plans how to implement them. That openness is a gift and a trap: teams that never vary the format eventually stop getting anything real out of it, which is the exact failure mode covered in [The Retro That Never Changes Anything](../06-real-life-scenarios/07-retro-that-never-changes-anything.md). Rotate through these deliberately rather than defaulting to whichever one you learned first.

## 1. Start / Stop / Continue

Three columns, one question each: what should we start doing that we aren't, what should we stop doing that isn't working, what should we continue doing because it's working. Each person adds sticky notes (physical or digital) to all three columns, the facilitator groups duplicates, and the team discusses and prioritizes.

**How to run it:** 5 minutes silent writing, 10-15 minutes grouping and clarifying, remaining time on discussion and picking 1-3 items to act on. Simple enough for a team's first retrospective ever, and durable enough to return to when you don't want to spend facilitation energy on a novel format.

## 2. Mad / Sad / Glad

Three columns by emotion instead of by action: what made you frustrated or angry this Sprint, what made you disappointed or discouraged, what made you happy or proud. Notes go up, get grouped, then get discussed — usually starting with Mad, since unaddressed frustration is the most likely thing to fester into disengagement if it goes unspoken.

**How to run it:** works best after a Sprint with real emotional texture — a rough one, a surprising win, an interpersonal friction point — because it gives people permission to name feelings that a purely process-focused format like Start/Stop/Continue tends to filter out. Facilitator should watch the room's energy in the Mad column specifically; if one person's sticky notes dominate it, that's worth a private follow-up regardless of what the group discussion produces.

## 3. 4Ls: Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed For

Four columns: what did we like about this Sprint, what did we learn (about the product, the tech, ourselves, the process), what did we lack (missing skills, tools, information, time), what did we long for (something we wished we'd had or done). Same silent-write-then-group flow as Start/Stop/Continue.

**How to run it:** the "Learned" column is the one most formats miss entirely, and it's often the most valuable for a team doing something genuinely new — a fresh tech stack, a first project with a new stakeholder group, a first attempt at a difficult integration. Good default for teams past their first few Sprints who've outgrown Start/Stop/Continue's simplicity but aren't in crisis.

## 4. Sailboat (Wind, Anchor, Rocks, Island)

A sailboat metaphor drawn on the board: the **island** is the goal or vision the team is sailing toward, the **wind** is what's pushing the team forward, the **anchor** is what's holding the team back, and **rocks** are risks ahead that could sink the boat if unaddressed. The team populates each area with sticky notes, then discusses.

**How to run it:** the visual metaphor makes this a strong choice for a team that's grown tired of column-and-sticky-note formats, and it forces a genuinely forward-looking element (rocks) that pure look-back formats like Mad/Sad/Glad don't naturally include. Draw the boat large and simple — this format lives or dies on whether people find it fun to interact with, not on artistic quality.

## 5. Timeline retro

The team reconstructs the Sprint chronologically on a horizontal timeline — each day or key event gets marked, and people add sticky notes above the line for positive moments and below the line for negative ones as they remember them. The team then walks the timeline together, discussing the clusters and turning points.

**How to run it:** best for a Sprint with a lot happening — an incident, a scope change, a hard deadline — where the sequence of events matters as much as the individual facts. It also surfaces things a simple category format misses, like "the Tuesday deploy is where everything started going sideways," because the timeline makes causality visible in a way isolated sticky notes don't.

## 6. 5 Whys (root cause)

Start from a single, specific, already-agreed-upon problem (not "things were bad" — something concrete, like "the payment integration bug reached production"). Ask "why did that happen?" and write the answer. Ask "why" again of that answer, and keep going, typically five times, until you reach something the team actually has the power to change — a missing test, an unclear ownership boundary, an assumption nobody checked — rather than stopping at the first surface-level cause.

**How to run it:** this is not a general-purpose retro format — it's a focused tool for one specific, well-defined problem, and trying to run an entire hour-long retrospective on 5 Whys alone usually means the team picked a problem too vague to actually drill into. Best paired with a broader format (run Start/Stop/Continue or Sailboat first to surface issues, then pick the sharpest one and 5-Why it). Stop when the "why" answers start repeating or the team reaches something genuinely outside their control — forcing a fifth "why" past that point produces fake precision, not insight.

## 7. Lean Coffee

No pre-set topics. Each person writes discussion topics on individual cards; the group silently dot-votes to order them. The team discusses the top topic with a short timer (5-7 minutes); when the timer ends, a quick thumbs-vote decides whether to continue the topic or move to the next one. Repeat until time runs out.

**How to run it:** the strongest format for a team with too much to talk about and no consensus on what matters most, because the prioritization is democratic and happens in the room rather than being decided by the facilitator beforehand. It also naturally self-limits — topics nobody actually cares about get voted off quickly instead of eating the full retro.

## 8. Appreciation round

No columns, no sticky notes. The team goes around (or opens the floor) and each person names something specific a teammate did during the Sprint that they're genuinely grateful for — not generic praise, a specific moment or action. Often run as a closing few minutes of a longer retro rather than the entire event.

**How to run it:** short — 10-15 minutes is enough — and best used either as a deliberate palate-cleanser after a hard Sprint, or folded onto the end of a heavier format like 5 Whys so the retro doesn't end on the most critical, deficit-focused note. Facilitator should model it first with a genuine, specific example, since a vague opener ("great job everyone") sets a low bar the rest of the room will match.

## 9. DAKI: Drop, Add, Keep, Improve

Four columns, more action-oriented than Start/Stop/Continue: what should we **drop** entirely (stop doing, full stop), what should we **add** (something new we're not doing at all), what should we **keep** (working well, don't touch it), what should we **improve** (working, but could work better). The Improve column is usually the largest and most nuanced — it's for things that are neither broken nor fine.

**How to run it:** useful when a team feels Start/Stop/Continue has gotten stale but wants a similarly quick, low-overhead format — DAKI covers similar ground with slightly more precision, especially the Drop/Improve distinction, which forces the team to separate "this is actively harmful" from "this is fine but could be sharper."

## 10. Team Radar / health check

The team scores itself, individually and anonymously if possible, on a fixed set of dimensions — common ones: Delivering Value, Suitable Process, Fun, Learning, Support, Pawns or Players (do we feel in control of our own work), Teamwork, Mission. Scores (1-5) get plotted on a radar/spider chart, and the team discusses the lowest-scoring dimensions and any dimension where individual scores diverge sharply.

**How to run it:** this is the closest thing to a quantitative pulse-check on the list, and it's most valuable run on a recurring cadence (every Sprint, or every few Sprints) so the team can see the radar shape change over time rather than treating any single session in isolation. A dimension that's consistently low across many cycles, or one where scores are wildly split between individuals, deserves a dedicated discussion even if it means shelving other topics that Sprint.

## Bonus: Starfish (Keep, More, Less, Stop, Start)

A five-point version of Start/Stop/Continue: **Keep doing**, **More of**, **Less of**, **Stop doing**, **Start doing**. The extra granularity between "more" and "keep," and between "less" and "stop," gives the team room to flag things that are almost right rather than forcing a binary judgment.

**How to run it:** same mechanics as Start/Stop/Continue — silent write, group, discuss — but reach for this one specifically when the team's feedback keeps landing in an awkward middle ground the three-column version can't capture ("this is working, but we're doing too much of it").

## Rotating formats to avoid retro fatigue

A team that runs the identical format every two weeks for six months will, predictably, start producing thinner and thinner output — not because the team has run out of problems, but because a repeated ritual stops prompting real reflection and starts producing the same three safe answers on autopilot. That's the mechanism behind [The Retro That Never Changes Anything](../06-real-life-scenarios/07-retro-that-never-changes-anything.md): the format isn't the root cause on its own, but a stale format is one of the easiest ways for a retrospective to become theater instead of inspection.

Practical rotation guidance:

- Change format roughly every 2-4 Sprints — often enough to keep the reflection genuine, rarely enough that the team gets fluent with each one instead of relearning the mechanics every time.
- Match the format to the Sprint that just happened (see the table below) rather than rotating on a fixed schedule regardless of context.
- Let the team choose the next format sometimes, not just the Scrum Master — ownership of the process is part of what the retrospective is supposed to be building.
- If a format produces genuinely nothing — no real disagreement, no new information, no action items anyone cares about — that's data. Ask the team directly: "did that format not fit this Sprint, or have we outgrown it?"

| The Sprint that just happened | Consider |
|---|---|
| Smooth, few surprises | 4Ls, Team Radar, Appreciation round |
| Rough, high emotion, conflict | Mad/Sad/Glad, Sailboat |
| One specific incident or recurring failure | 5 Whys |
| Too many issues, no clear priority | Lean Coffee |
| Team feels the retro itself has gone stale | Starfish, DAKI, Timeline retro |
| First few retros with a brand-new team | Start/Stop/Continue |

## Every retro ends with owned, trackable action items

Whatever format you run, a retrospective that ends without concrete follow-through is indistinguishable from one that never happened — the Scrum Guide is explicit that the Sprint's most impactful improvement gets addressed as soon as possible, and can even be added to the Sprint Backlog directly. Before closing any retrospective, force every discussion topic that produced a decision through this template:

```
Action:      [specific, observable — "add a staging smoke test to the deploy pipeline,"
              not "improve testing"]
Owner:       [one named person, not "the team"]
Due by:      [a date or Sprint, not "soon"]
How we'll know it worked: [what changes, and where it'll be visible]
```

- [ ] Every action has exactly one owner — a shared or team-wide owner is how action items quietly die.
- [ ] Every action is specific enough that a stranger reading it later would know whether it happened.
- [ ] No more than 1-3 action items leave any single retro — a list of nine improvements is a list nobody does.
- [ ] Last Sprint's action items get reviewed at the *start* of this retro before generating new ones — an item that's been carried three Sprints running is itself worth discussing.
- [ ] At least one action item that's fully within the team's own control makes it onto the list, even when a bigger organizational impediment also came up — the team should never leave a retro with nothing they can do themselves.
