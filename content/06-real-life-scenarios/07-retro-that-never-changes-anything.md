# The Retro That Never Changes Anything

*Same format, same sticky notes, same three complaints, every two weeks, for the last five months — and nothing has actually changed.*

## The Situation

Team Halyard, seven developers building a fleet-management dashboard, has run "Start / Stop / Continue" as its Retrospective format every Sprint for five months — 11 Retrospectives in a row. The Scrum Master, Behnam, pulls up his notes from Sprint 6 and Sprint 16 side by side out of curiosity, and finds nearly identical sticky notes on both boards: "Stop: too many meetings scheduled during focus time." "Stop: QA environment is unreliable, wastes half a day most Sprints." "Start: better handoff notes between backend and frontend."

Ten Sprints apart, same three complaints, worded almost identically. In Sprint 16's retro, one developer, Julieta, says out loud what several others are visibly thinking: "Why are we even doing this if nothing changes? I could write today's board from memory before we start." Two developers have started treating retro as an optional meeting they "have a conflict" for roughly every third Sprint.

## Why This Happens

A retrospective that produces the same complaints Sprint after Sprint isn't failing because the team lacks insight — Team Halyard clearly knows exactly what's wrong, in detail, repeatedly. It's failing at the step after insight:

- **Action items get generated but never get owned, sized, or tracked as real work.** "Improve handoff notes" gets written on a sticky note and then evaporates the moment everyone returns to their desks, because it was never assigned to a person, given a concrete next step, or checked on at the following retro.
- **The QA-environment complaint is an organizational impediment, not a team-fixable process tweak**, and the team has been writing it on a sticky note as if repeating it enough times will make infrastructure budget appear. Nobody has escalated it as the impediment it actually is.
- **Format fatigue sets in when the same three questions get asked in the same order for months.** "Start / Stop / Continue" is a fine format, but running it unchanged for 11 Sprints in a row trains the team to pattern-match the meeting rather than actually reflect — people write what they remember writing last time.
- **Nobody is closing the loop.** The single most corrosive thing happening here is that the *first* five minutes of a retro are never "here's what we said we'd do last time, and here's what actually happened." Without that check, there's no accountability loop, and without an accountability loop, a retrospective is theater — it produces the emotional beat of having addressed a problem without any of the substance.

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Open the very next retro with a retrospective of the retrospectives.** Behnam brings the Sprint 6 and Sprint 16 boards up side by side, visible to everyone, and asks the room directly: "These are ten Sprints apart. What does this tell us?" Letting the team see the repetition themselves lands harder than Behnam simply announcing it — this is inspection applied to the inspection mechanism itself.
2. **Split the backlog of complaints by who can actually act on them.** Behnam facilitates sorting "too many meetings during focus time" and "handoff notes" (team-controllable) from "QA environment is unreliable" (needs infrastructure budget/access outside the team's authority) — because treating an organizational impediment as if it's a team habit problem is exactly why it's been reappearing for five months with no motion.
3. **Escalate the QA-environment item as a formal, tracked impediment, not another sticky note.** Behnam takes it to whoever owns infrastructure budget, with the concrete cost attached — "half a day lost most Sprints, times seven developers, for at least five months" — and reports back to the team on real progress at the next retro, whether that progress is "fixed" or "still blocked, here's why." See [removing impediments](../04-scrum-master-role/removing-impediments.md) — the Scrum Master's job includes escalating blockers the team cannot clear itself, visibly and persistently, not just facilitating a meeting where they get named.
4. **Change the format, deliberately, to break the pattern-matching reflex.** Behnam retires "Start / Stop / Continue" for a few Sprints in favor of something structurally different — a "Sailboat" retro one Sprint, a focused single-topic deep-dive the next (just the handoff-notes problem, nothing else, for the full hour). A new format forces people to actually think again instead of recalling last time's answer. See [retrospective formats](../08-templates-and-toolkit/retrospective-formats.md) for a working rotation.
5. **Make every retro end with owned, sized actions — not themes.** Behnam changes the closing ritual: every retro must produce at most two action items, each with a named owner and a concrete first step ("Rana will draft a one-page handoff template by Wednesday and share it in Slack for feedback" — not "improve handoffs"). Fewer, owned, concrete actions beat a long list of vague good intentions every time.
6. **Open every subsequent retro by reviewing last Sprint's actions before generating new ones.** This is the single highest-leverage change: five minutes at the start of each retro, checking what was committed to last time and what actually happened, closes the loop that's been open for five months. If an action didn't happen, that itself becomes the first topic of discussion — why not, and what actually blocked it.

## Likely Outcome

The handoff-notes template ships within a week because it finally has an owner and a deadline instead of being everyone's vague responsibility, which means no one's. The QA-environment issue takes six weeks and two escalation follow-ups to get budget approved — slower than the team would like, but visibly moving for the first time in five months, which changes the room's mood even before it's fully resolved. Retro attendance stops being something people find conflicts for once people see actions actually land; Julieta, the developer who called out the theater, becomes one of the most engaged participants once she sees her Sprint-6 complaint actually addressed in Sprint 18.

## Certification Exam Angle

A common situational question: *"A team has run the same Retrospective format for many consecutive Sprints, and the same issues keep being raised without resolution. What should the Scrum Master do?"* The trap answer is often "cancel the Retrospective since it isn't producing value" — this is a serious misread; a Retrospective event failing to drive improvement is a signal to fix *how* the event is being facilitated, not evidence the event itself is unnecessary — the Scrum Guide treats the Retrospective as mandatory precisely because continuous improvement doesn't happen by accident.

A second trap answer: "the Scrum Master should personally decide and implement the fixes for the recurring issues" — tempting because it looks efficient, but it removes the team's ownership of its own improvement, and the QA-environment example shows why that's specifically wrong here: it needs organizational escalation, not a unilateral fix, and the *habit-based* items need the team's own commitment to change, not a mandate from outside. The correct-mindset answer diagnoses why actions aren't sticking (no ownership, no follow-up, impediments outside the team's control being treated as if they're inside it), fixes the facilitation mechanics — varied formats, owned actions, closing the loop each time — and separately escalates anything genuinely outside the team's authority, because a Scrum Master's job is to make the Retrospective's *inspect-and-adapt* mechanism actually work, not to become the mechanism themselves.
