# A Distributed Team Across Timezones

*Six developers in Dhaka, three in Austin, and a two-hour daily overlap window that's shrinking, not growing.*

## The Situation

Team Falcon builds the reporting module for a US-headquartered SaaS company. Six developers and a QA lead sit in Dhaka (UTC+6); three developers and the Product Owner, Marissa, sit in Austin (UTC-5, or UTC-6 during US winter). The overlap window is 7:00–9:00 p.m. Dhaka time, which is 8:00–10:00 a.m. in Austin — and it's currently the only two hours of the day both halves of the team are reliably awake and online.

The Scrum Master, Nabila, based in Dhaka, has been running Daily Scrum at 7:00 p.m. Dhaka time for two months. Attendance from Austin has quietly dropped from all three developers to one. In Sprint Review, the Austin contingent's feedback increasingly arrives as Slack messages the next morning rather than live in the meeting, by which point the Dhaka team has already moved on to Planning. A recent Sprint slipped because a Dhaka developer built against an API contract Marissa had verbally changed her mind about in an Austin-only conversation that never made it back across the overlap window.

## Why This Happens

Timezone-split teams don't fail because of distance — plenty of fully async open-source projects work fine across a dozen timezones. They fail because Scrum's default event cadence assumes everyone can be in the same room at the same time, and nobody explicitly redesigned the events for a reality where that's only true for two hours a day:

- **A single synchronous Daily Scrum time is structurally unfair to whichever half of the team it's inconvenient for**, and inconvenience compounds — 7:00 p.m. is a mild ask once; it's a corrosive ask five nights a week for months, and attendance decay is the predictable result, not a discipline failure.
- **Information generated outside the overlap window doesn't automatically make it back into the shared record.** Marissa's scope change happened in an Austin hallway conversation with no Dhaka developer present and no written trace — the kind of informal update that's harmless on a co-located team because someone overhears it, and dangerous on a distributed one because nobody does.
- **Events that assume real-time discussion (Sprint Review feedback, Retrospective dot-voting) degrade badly when half the participants are asynchronous latecomers** — by the time Austin's written feedback lands, Dhaka has already moved to the next ceremony and the input arrives too late to act on.
- **"We have a daily standup" gets mistaken for "we have working cross-timezone communication,"** when in practice a fifteen-minute synchronous window can carry status updates but can't carry the deeper design discussions, clarifications, and course-corrections a team actually needs — those need a different mechanism entirely.

## The Scrum Master's Approach

1. **Treat the overlap window as the team's scarcest resource and protect it accordingly.** Nabila stops using the two-hour window for a routine status-recitation Daily Scrum and reserves it for the highest-value synchronous work: Sprint Planning, Sprint Review, and any blocking design decisions that genuinely need real-time back-and-forth. Fifteen minutes of pure status doesn't need two humans awake at inconvenient hours; it needs a channel.
2. **Redesign Daily Scrum as asynchronous-first, with a synchronous slot layered on top only when needed.** Nabila sets up a written Daily Scrum in a shared channel — same three questions, posted by every developer by a fixed time in their own morning — and reserves five minutes of the overlap window purely for anyone to flag "I need to talk live about this," rather than making the full meeting synchronous by default. See [Daily Scrum](../02-scrum-framework/daily-scrum.md) for what the event is actually for — inspection and a plan for the next 24 hours doesn't require simultaneity, only visibility.
3. **Rotate the inconvenience instead of parking it permanently on one side.** For the events that do need to be synchronous — Planning and Review — Nabila alternates start times every other Sprint between "Dhaka-friendly" and "Austin-friendly" slots, so the cost of distributed work is shared rather than quietly absorbed by whichever office has less organizational power to push back.
4. **Make the written record the source of truth, not the hallway conversation.** Nabila works with Marissa to establish a hard norm: any Product Backlog or scope decision, even one first discussed verbally, isn't real until it's written in the shared backlog tool with a timestamp. The API-contract incident becomes the concrete example Nabila uses to make this land — not as a rule imposed from nowhere, but as the direct fix to a failure everyone just watched happen.
5. **Use the overlap window's Retrospective time deliberately, and let async input feed it rather than trail it.** Nabila switches the Retrospective format to open an async input document 24 hours before the live session — both offices add points on their own time — so the live overlap-window portion is spent discussing and deciding, not waiting for people to think of things out loud. See [retrospective formats](../08-templates-and-toolkit/retrospective-formats.md) for async-friendly structures.
6. **Actively facilitate relationship-building, not just task coordination.** Nabila proposes a standing informal 15 minutes at the start of the monthly Planning overlap session with no agenda — because distributed teams that only ever talk about tickets tend to under-trust each other exactly when a hard trade-off conversation requires trust to go well.

## Likely Outcome

Async Daily Scrum attendance normalizes to effectively 100% within two Sprints, because it costs nobody an inconvenient evening or morning. Live overlap-window attendance for Planning and Review improves once it's clearly the only synchronous event and its time rotates — Austin developers stop treating the window as optional once it's not competing with a nightly ritual. The written-decision norm prevents a repeat of the API-contract incident, though it takes visible enforcement (Nabila gently asking "is this in the backlog yet?" in Slack) for a few weeks before it sticks without prompting. Nabila's own workload shifts — less live facilitation, more curating and connecting written threads across timezones — which is a real skill shift, not a lesser one.

## Certification Exam Angle

A situational question here often reads: *"A Scrum Team is split across two timezones with limited overlap, and remote members increasingly disengage from Scrum events. What should the Scrum Master do?"* The trap answer is usually "require all team members to attend all events live, adjusting personal schedules as needed" — this sounds like upholding discipline but actually guarantees resentment and attrition of engagement from whichever half absorbs the cost, and ignores that Scrum's events describe a purpose, not a mandated delivery mechanism.

Another trap answer: "since synchronous collaboration isn't feasible, drop Daily Scrum for this team" — this overcorrects; the event's purpose (daily inspection and adaptation toward the Sprint Goal) doesn't stop being necessary just because the format needs to change. The correct-mindset answer redesigns *how* the events achieve their purpose for the team's real constraints — asynchronous inspection where synchronicity isn't essential, protected and rotated synchronous time where it is — which reflects the Scrum Guide's own framing that Scrum's events, roles, and artifacts have immutable purposes but adaptable implementations.
