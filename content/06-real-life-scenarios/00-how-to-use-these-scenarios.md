# How to Use These Scenarios

*A short preface on the format of this section and how to study it — read this before jumping into scenario 01.*

Every other section of this guide teaches you what Scrum *is*. This section teaches you what to *do* when it's Tuesday afternoon, the Sprint is on fire, and the Scrum Guide's thirteen pages don't tell you how to handle the specific human being standing in front of you demanding an answer.

## The format

Every scenario in this section follows the same five-part structure, on purpose — so you can learn to pattern-match it under exam pressure and under real pressure:

1. **The Situation** — a concrete scene: a named team, a sprint number, a piece of dialogue, a number that makes it real (velocity, team size, sprint length). Not an abstraction. If you can't picture the standup, we haven't done our job.
2. **Why This Happens** — the underlying mechanism. Almost nothing in this section is a one-off bad-luck event. Missed Sprint Goals, status-report standups, tech-debt standoffs — these are structural failure modes that recur across teams and companies because the same pressures produce them. Understanding the mechanism is what lets you recognize the pattern next time it's wearing a different costume.
3. **The Scrum Master's Approach** — numbered, concrete steps. Not "facilitate a conversation" — actual sentences you could say, actual artifacts you'd change, actual sequence of who you'd talk to first and why.
4. **Likely Outcome** — what actually tends to happen when you run the approach, including the parts that don't fully resolve in one Sprint. Real teams don't heal in one retrospective; this section is honest about that.
5. **Certification Exam Angle** — how Scrum.org (PSM I/II) or Scrum Alliance (CSM/A-CSM) would turn this exact situation into a scored question, what the correct-mindset answer looks like, and what the tempting-but-wrong answer looks like and why it's a trap.

## Why scenario-based thinking is the actual skill

The Scrum Guide is deliberately short — Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland kept trimming it edition over edition precisely because Scrum is a minimal framework, not a rulebook with an answer for every situation. That minimalism is the point: Scrum gives you roles, events, artifacts, and a handful of values, and trusts you to apply empiricism — transparency, inspection, adaptation — to whatever mess is actually in front of you.

That means the real job of a Scrum Master isn't reciting the definition of a Sprint Review. It's pattern recognition under ambiguity: a manager sits in on Daily Scrum and the team starts reporting to him instead of each other — what's actually going wrong here, and which of six plausible interventions is the one that fixes the cause instead of the symptom? Nobody hands you a flowchart. You have to have already thought about it once, ideally before it happens to you at 9:14 a.m. with your stakeholder's coffee still steaming.

This is also, not coincidentally, exactly how Scrum.org and Scrum Alliance write their certification exams. PSM I and PSM II are not "define a Sprint" quizzes — the majority of questions are situational: a paragraph describing a team's specific dysfunction, followed by four answers that are all *plausible-sounding Scrum Master actions*, where only one is consistent with the Scrum values and the Scrum Master's actual accountabilities, and the other three are exactly the instinctive, well-meaning, wrong things most people would do under pressure (jump in and fix it yourself, escalate to a manager, add a rule, cancel the ceremony). You cannot memorize your way to a passing score on PSM II. You have to have internalized the mindset well enough to spot the trap answers on sight. That's what this section trains.

## How to actually study this section

Don't read these scenarios like articles. Read them like practice exams:

1. **Read only "The Situation."** Stop there. Do not scroll further yet.
2. **Pause and write your own answer** — on paper, in a notes app, anywhere — before you read a single word of "The Scrum Master's Approach." Force yourself to commit to what you'd actually say and do, in order. This step is the entire value of the exercise; skipping it turns active practice into passive reading, and passive reading does not transfer to an exam room or a standup.
3. **Then read "Why This Happens"** and check whether your answer addressed the root cause or just the symptom you saw on the surface.
4. **Compare your answer to "The Scrum Master's Approach."** Where do they diverge? Did you jump straight to a fix instead of first making the problem visible? Did you escalate something the team should own? Did you protect the team from a stakeholder in a way that was actually enabling avoidance rather than shielding?
5. **Read "Certification Exam Angle" last, every time**, even if you're not currently studying for a certification. It's the fastest way to see your own instinct reflected back as either the correct-mindset answer or the tempting trap — and recognizing which one you reached for is more useful than any flashcard.

If you're preparing for [PSM I or PSM II](../07-certifications/scrum-org/), or [CSM or A-CSM](../07-certifications/scrum-alliance/), treat this entire section as your situational-question bank — work through it start to finish, then come back to the ones you got wrong a second time a week later. If you're doing the job right now and landed on this page because your Daily Scrum has quietly turned into a status meeting, skip straight to the scenario that matches your Tuesday. Both uses are intended. Start with [scenario 01](01-team-missing-every-sprint-goal.md).
