# Scrum Values

*The five values — Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, and Courage — that determine whether Scrum's events and artifacts actually function, or just become theater performed on a schedule.*

The 2020 Scrum Guide is thirteen pages, and it spends real estate on five words that don't sound like process at all: Commitment, Focus, Openness, Respect, Courage. That's deliberate. Scrum gives you a skeleton — three roles, five events, three artifacts — but the skeleton moves or doesn't move depending on whether the people inside it actually behave a certain way. Two teams can run byte-for-byte identical ceremonies and produce completely different outcomes, because one team lives these values and the other is just going through the motions. The Guide is explicit that successful use of Scrum depends on people getting *better* at living these five values over time — they aren't a one-time onboarding slide, they're a practice.

Here's what each one looks like when it's actually present, and what its absence looks like in a real team — not abstractly, but in behavior you could observe in a single Sprint.

## Commitment

The Scrum Team commits to achieving its goals and to supporting one another while doing it. Concretely: Developers commit to a Sprint Goal in Sprint Planning and treat it as a real target, not a wish list. The Product Owner commits to keeping the Product Backlog in a state the team can actually plan against. The Scrum Master commits to the process working, not just existing.

**Present:** a Developer who finishes their own tasks early picks up something else toward the Sprint Goal instead of starting unrelated work. A blocked teammate gets help before the Daily Scrum even asks for it.

**Absent:** Sprints get treated as a rolling list of individual tickets with no shared target — everyone hits their own tasks and the Sprint Goal (if one was even written down) quietly fails, and nobody feels responsible because "my part was done."

## Focus

The primary focus is the work of the Sprint, aimed at making the best possible progress toward its goals. This is the value most directly under attack in most companies, because organizations love to interrupt.

**Present:** a team that pushes back on a mid-Sprint "quick request" from another department, or at minimum surfaces it explicitly to the Product Owner instead of just quietly absorbing it. Work-in-progress limits are respected. The Daily Scrum keeps returning to "what does the Sprint Goal need."

**Absent:** Developers are staffed across three Sprints in three different products simultaneously, context-switching constantly, and nobody can tell you what this Sprint's goal is without looking it up.

## Openness

The Scrum Team and its stakeholders agree to be open about the work and the challenges with performing the work. Openness is what makes [Transparency](scrum-theory-pillars.md) possible as a lived behavior, not just a board layout.

**Present:** a Developer says on day two of the Sprint that an approach isn't working, instead of quietly grinding until day eight. A Product Owner tells stakeholders plainly, before Sprint Review even starts, that the Sprint Goal is at risk.

**Absent:** status is sandbagged so nothing looks wrong until it's too late to fix. A broken build gets patched over quietly instead of raised. Dashboards say green because saying yellow feels like admitting failure.

## Respect

Scrum Team members respect each other to be capable, independent people. This is the value that makes self-management possible at all — you cannot ask people to self-manage and then override every decision they make.

**Present:** the Scrum Master doesn't tell Developers how to do their work, and leadership doesn't overrule the Product Owner's ordering of the Product Backlog without going through them. Disagreement in a Retrospective is aimed at the problem, not the person.

**Absent:** a manager assigns tasks directly to individual Developers, bypassing the team's own planning. A Scrum Master dictates *how* a technical problem should be solved. Teammates talk over each other or dismiss ideas based on who said them rather than what was said.

## Courage

Scrum Team members have the courage to do the right thing and to work on tough problems. Courage is the value that turns the other four from good intentions into actual behavior, because openness, focus, and respect all cost something in the moment.

**Present:** a Product Owner says no to a powerful stakeholder's pet feature because it doesn't serve the Product Goal. A Developer flags, out loud, that a shortcut will create technical debt the team will pay for in two Sprints. A Scrum Master names a dysfunction in the room instead of letting it slide because naming it is awkward.

**Absent:** everyone sees the same problem — a recurring blocker, an unrealistic deadline, a stakeholder who keeps changing scope mid-Sprint — and nobody says anything, Sprint after Sprint.

## Why the Guide ties the values to the pillars

The Guide's own framing is that when these five values are embodied by the Scrum Team and the people they work with, the three pillars of empirical process control — [Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation](scrum-theory-pillars.md) — come to life and build trust. That's not a throwaway line; it's causal. Transparency requires Openness and Courage, because someone has to be willing to say the true, uncomfortable thing out loud. Inspection requires Focus and Commitment, because looking honestly at your own work takes attention you'd rather spend elsewhere. Adaptation requires Respect and Courage, because acting on what inspection reveals often means admitting the current plan, or your own prior decision, was wrong.

Without the values, the pillars still exist as mechanics — a board still gets updated, a Daily Scrum still happens, a Retrospective still gets scheduled — but they stop doing the thing they exist to do. A Sprint Backlog updated by someone who isn't being Open is just decoration. A Retrospective run by a team without Courage produces a list of surface complaints and no real change. This is why "doing the ceremonies" and "doing Scrum" are not the same thing, and it's the single biggest thing a [Scrum Master's servant leadership](../04-scrum-master-role/servant-leadership.md) is trying to build in a team — not compliance with the framework, but the values that make the framework worth anything.
