# The Scrum Team

*One team, three accountabilities — Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers — small enough to stay nimble, cross-functional enough to need no one outside it to get the work done.*

The 2020 Scrum Guide is precise about a distinction that a lot of teams still get wrong: there is one Scrum Team, and it has three accountabilities inside it — Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers. There is no "Development Team" as a separate entity sitting underneath a Product Owner and Scrum Master; that framing existed in earlier versions of the Guide and was deliberately removed. If you want the history of why that changed and what it replaced, that belongs in the guide's [history of Scrum](../01-agile-foundations/history-of-scrum.md) — what matters here is current practice: one team, three peer accountabilities, no internal hierarchy.

## The shape of the team

The Scrum Team is cross-functional, meaning the members collectively have all the skills necessary to create value each Sprint, and self-managing, meaning they internally decide who does what, when, and how — without anyone outside the team directing that. There are no sub-teams or hierarchies within it. The whole Scrum Team is accountable for creating a valuable, useful Increment every Sprint.

Size guidance: Scrum Teams are usually 10 or fewer people total, small enough to stay nimble and large enough to get meaningful work done within a Sprint. This is a total-team number, not a per-role or per-Developer count — it covers the Product Owner and Scrum Master too. Teams that grow past this tend to need a scaling framework rather than one larger Scrum Team; that's covered in [Scaling Frameworks](../05-scaling-frameworks/).

## Product Owner

The Product Owner is accountable for maximizing the value of the product that results from the Scrum Team's work. In practice, that accountability is exercised entirely through effective management of the [Product Backlog](product-backlog.md):

- Developing and clearly communicating the Product Goal.
- Creating and clearly communicating Product Backlog items.
- Ordering Product Backlog items — deciding what matters most, next.
- Ensuring the Product Backlog is transparent, visible, and understood by the Scrum Team.

The Product Owner may delegate the actual work of any of this to Developers, but the accountability stays with them — delegating the task doesn't delegate the outcome. The Product Owner is one person, not a committee, even when representing the interests of many stakeholders. They don't need to satisfy every stakeholder's request; their real decisions show up as the order of the Product Backlog, and that order is theirs to set.

For a Product Owner to actually succeed, the rest of the organization has to respect their decisions in a very concrete way: nobody gets to hand Developers a different set of requirements, and Developers don't act on direction from anyone the Product Owner hasn't sanctioned. A Product Owner whose ordering gets routinely overridden by a VP isn't really accountable for value — they're a scribe.

## Scrum Master

The Scrum Master is accountable for establishing Scrum as described in the Scrum Guide, which in practice means being accountable for the Scrum Team's effectiveness. They are a true leader who serves the Scrum Team and the larger organization — not a project manager, not the Developers' boss, and not accountable for the product itself (that's the Product Owner) or for doing the work (that's the Developers).

The Guide breaks this service into three categories:

**Service to the Product Owner** — helping find techniques for effective Product Goal definition and Product Backlog management; helping the Scrum Team understand the need for clear, concise Product Backlog items; helping establish empirical product planning in a complex environment; facilitating stakeholder collaboration as requested or needed.

**Service to the Developers** — coaching them in self-management and cross-functionality; helping them focus on creating high-value Increments that meet the Definition of Done; causing the removal of impediments to their progress; ensuring all Scrum events take place, stay positive and productive, and are kept within their timebox.

**Service to the Organization** — leading, training, and coaching the organization in its Scrum adoption; planning and advising Scrum implementations across the organization; helping employees and stakeholders understand and enact an empirical, incremental approach to complex work; removing barriers between stakeholders and Scrum Teams.

That three-way split is worth internalizing because it's the actual shape of the job, not a soft description of "facilitator." It's covered in far more operational depth in [Servant Leadership](../04-scrum-master-role/servant-leadership.md) and [Facilitation Techniques](../04-scrum-master-role/facilitation-techniques.md) — this page is the accountability as the Guide defines it; those pages are how it's done day to day.

## Developers

Developers are the people in the Scrum Team who are committed to creating any aspect of a usable Increment each Sprint. "Developers" is a Scrum accountability, not a job title — it covers whoever is doing the hands-on work of building the product, regardless of their functional specialty (engineering, testing, design, data, writing — whatever the product actually needs). Scrum itself doesn't recognize sub-roles like "QA" or "architect" within this accountability; the skills differ, the accountability doesn't.

Developers are specifically accountable for:

- Creating the plan for the Sprint — the [Sprint Backlog](sprint-backlog.md).
- Instilling quality by adhering to the team's [Definition of Done](definition-of-done.md).
- Adapting their plan every day toward the Sprint Goal.
- Holding each other accountable as professionals.

That last point matters more than it sounds like it should: Scrum deliberately doesn't give the Scrum Master or Product Owner authority to hold individual Developers accountable for their work. That accountability sits inside the Developer group itself, which is exactly what "self-managing" means in practice, not just in the Guide's vocabulary.

## Why "one team" is the load-bearing idea

Almost every Scrum dysfunction traces back to someone treating this as three separate teams instead of one. A Product Owner who thinks the Developers work *for* them. A Scrum Master who thinks they manage the Developers. Developers who think their job ends at "did what the ticket said" and the Product Goal is someone else's problem. The Guide's insistence on *one* Scrum Team, with three accountabilities inside it rather than a hierarchy, is what the [Scrum Values](scrum-values.md) — especially Respect and Commitment — are meant to protect. See [Sprint Planning](sprint-planning.md) and [Daily Scrum](daily-scrum.md) for how these three accountabilities actually interact inside the events, and [Scrum Theory: The Three Pillars](scrum-theory-pillars.md) for why this structure exists at all.
