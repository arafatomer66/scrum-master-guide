# Glossary

*A-Z reference for Scrum, Agile, and scaling terminology used throughout this guide — one precise definition per term, not a tutorial.*

This page is a lookup tool, not a teaching page — for depth on any term, follow its link into the relevant section: the [Scrum Framework](02-scrum-framework/) for core Scrum Guide terms, [Professional Scrum Competencies](03-professional-scrum-competencies/00-overview.md) for the metrics and evidence-based terms, and [Scaling Frameworks](05-scaling-frameworks/) for Nexus, Scrum@Scale, LeSS, and SAFe vocabulary.

## A

**Acceptance Criteria** — The specific, testable conditions a single Product Backlog item must satisfy to be considered complete, agreed between the Developers and the Product Owner before or during Sprint Planning. Distinct from the Definition of Done, which applies to every Increment regardless of which item produced it.

**Adaptation** — The third pillar of empiricism in Scrum: adjusting a process, plan, or product as soon as inspection reveals it's off target. Adaptation without honest inspection is just guessing; inspection without adaptation is theater.

**Agile Manifesto** — The 2001 statement of four values and twelve principles (individuals and interactions over processes and tools, working software over comprehensive documentation, customer collaboration over contract negotiation, responding to change over following a plan) that Scrum, Kanban, XP, and every other Agile framework trace their lineage back to.

**Agile Release Train (ART)** — SAFe's core delivery vehicle: a long-lived group of typically 5-12 Agile teams (50-125 people) that plan, commit to, and deliver together on a synchronized cadence. See [SAFe Overview](05-scaling-frameworks/safe-overview.md).

**Artifact (Scrum Artifact)** — One of Scrum's three formal work products — the Product Backlog, the Sprint Backlog, and the Increment — each carrying a commitment (Product Goal, Sprint Goal, Definition of Done respectively) that gives it transparency and a target to inspect against.

## B

**Backlog Refinement** — The ongoing activity of adding detail, estimates, and order to Product Backlog items, done continuously rather than in one dedicated event — not a formal Scrum event, but an activity the Scrum Guide expects throughout the Sprint.

**Burndown Chart** — A chart tracking remaining work (story points, hours, or item count) against time, sloping toward zero if a team is on pace to finish; commonly used at the Sprint level, though the Scrum Guide itself doesn't mandate any specific chart.

**Burnup Chart** — A chart tracking completed work accumulating upward against a (sometimes moving) scope line — preferred by many practitioners over a burndown because it visibly separates "work done" from "scope changed," which a burndown conflates into one downward line.

## C

**Commitment (Scrum value)** — One of Scrum's five values: the Scrum Team personally commits to achieving its goals and to supporting each other. Distinct from a fixed-scope promise — a Sprint Goal is a commitment to an outcome, not a guarantee of every planned item.

**Conway's Law** — The observation that any organization designing a system will produce a design that mirrors its own communication structure. Central to deciding team boundaries before choosing a scaling framework — see [Why and When to Scale](05-scaling-frameworks/why-and-when-to-scale.md).

**Courage (Scrum value)** — One of Scrum's five values: the Scrum Team has the courage to do the right thing, work on tough problems, and be transparent even when the truth is uncomfortable.

**Cross-functional Team** — A team that holds every skill needed to go from a Product Backlog item to a done Increment without depending on anyone outside the team — the structural property Scrum assumes of the Developers, and the property component teams typically lack.

**Cumulative Flow Diagram (CFD)** — A stacked-area chart showing how many work items sit in each workflow state (to do, in progress, done) over time; a widening band signals work piling up in that state, a core diagnostic tool in Kanban and flow-based measurement.

**Cycle Time** — The elapsed time from when work actually starts on an item to when it's done — a core flow metric, and generally a more actionable signal than velocity because it measures a single item's journey rather than a team's aggregate estimate.

## D

**Daily Scrum** — The 15-minute, Developers-only event held every day of the Sprint to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and adapt the Sprint Backlog as needed. It belongs to the Developers, not the Scrum Master, and is not a status report to anyone outside the team.

**Definition of Done (DoD)** — The shared, product-wide quality standard every Increment must meet to be considered releasable, regardless of which Product Backlog item produced it. If a Scrum Team has no organizational standard imposed on it, the Scrum Team creates its own.

**Definition of Ready (DoR)** — A team-level (not Scrum Guide-mandated) checklist for when a Product Backlog item is well-enough understood to be pulled into a Sprint — useful in practice, but a common source of over-formalized gatekeeping if applied too rigidly.

**Developers** — The Scrum Guide's term (since the 2020 update) for the people on the Scrum Team who commit to creating any aspect of a usable Increment each Sprint — replaces the older, less precise term "development team members." See [The Scrum Team](02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md).

## E

**Empiricism** — The core philosophy Scrum is built on: knowledge comes from experience, and decisions are made based on what is observed, not what is predicted in advance. Operates through three pillars — transparency, inspection, adaptation.

**Epic** — A large body of work too big to fit in a single Sprint, typically broken down into smaller Product Backlog items before it's actually pulled into a Sprint. Not a formal Scrum Guide term, but common practice vocabulary.

**Evidence-Based Management (EBM)** — Scrum.org's framework for making organizational and product decisions based on empirical measures of value delivered rather than assumptions or output volume; central to the [Professional Scrum Competencies](03-professional-scrum-competencies/00-overview.md) model.

**Executive Action Team (EAT)** — In Scrum@Scale, the small senior-leadership body accountable for removing organizational impediments beyond a single team's or Scrum of Scrums's authority, and for continuously improving the Scrum@Scale implementation itself. See [Scrum@Scale](05-scaling-frameworks/scrum-at-scale.md).

**Executive MetaScrum (EMS)** — In Scrum@Scale, the forum where Product Owners and executive stakeholders align on a single, prioritized, organization-wide strategic backlog and resolve cross-team prioritization conflicts. See [Scrum@Scale](05-scaling-frameworks/scrum-at-scale.md).

**Extreme Programming (XP)** — An Agile framework predating and contemporaneous with Scrum, focused specifically on engineering practices (pair programming, test-driven development, continuous integration, collective code ownership) that many Scrum teams borrow regardless of which framework they formally follow.

## F

**Feature Team** — A cross-functional, cross-component team organized around delivering customer-facing value end to end, as opposed to a component team scoped to a single architectural layer. The team shape both LeSS and Nexus assume as a prerequisite for scaling well.

**Flow** — In Kanban and EBM thinking, the smooth, unimpeded movement of work items through a system from start to done; measured through cycle time, throughput, and work in progress rather than through story-point velocity.

**Focus (Scrum value)** — One of Scrum's five values: everyone focuses on the work of the Sprint and the goals of the Scrum Team, rather than splitting attention across unrelated work.

## I

**Impediment** — Anything that slows or blocks a team's progress toward its Sprint Goal or ongoing effectiveness — a broken build pipeline, an unavailable dependency team, an unresolved organizational policy. Removing or escalating impediments is a core Scrum Master accountability.

**Increment** — A concrete, working step toward the Product Goal, produced during a Sprint and meeting the Definition of Done; each Increment is additive to all prior Increments and must be usable.

**Inspect & Adapt (I&A) Workshop** — In SAFe, the end-of-Program-Increment event combining a System Demo, a quantitative/qualitative measures review, and a structured root-cause problem-solving workshop — SAFe's train-level equivalent of a Sprint Retrospective. See [SAFe Overview](05-scaling-frameworks/safe-overview.md).

**Inspection** — The second pillar of empiricism in Scrum: frequently examining artifacts and progress toward a goal to detect undesirable variances or problems, done through the Scrum events themselves rather than through separate inspection meetings.

**Iteration** — A generic Agile term for a fixed, repeating work cycle; "Sprint" is Scrum's specific name for its iteration, but not every iterative framework uses Scrum's exact rules.

## K

**Kanban** — A flow-based Agile method (distinct from Scrum) that visualizes work on a board, limits work in progress, and manages flow continuously rather than through fixed-length iterations; many Scrum teams borrow its board and WIP-limit practices without adopting it wholesale.

**Kanban Board** — A visual board with columns representing workflow states (e.g., To Do, In Progress, Review, Done) that cards move across left to right; used both in pure Kanban and inside Scrum teams to visualize the Sprint Backlog.

## L

**Lead Time** — The elapsed time from when a work item is requested (or added to the backlog) to when it's delivered — broader than cycle time, which measures only the active-work portion of that same journey.

**Lean** — The manufacturing-derived management philosophy (originating with the Toyota Production System) emphasizing eliminating waste, amplifying learning, and delivering value fast — a direct intellectual ancestor of both Kanban and much of Scrum's thinking.

**LeSS (Large-Scale Scrum)** — Bas Vodde and Craig Larman's scaling framework, built on descaling organizational complexity rather than adding process: one Product Backlog, one Product Owner, one Definition of Done across up to roughly 8 teams (LeSS Huge beyond that). See [LeSS](05-scaling-frameworks/less.md).

## M

**Minimum Marketable Feature (MMF)** — The smallest slice of functionality that's both usable and valuable enough to actually ship and market on its own — a scoping discipline related to, but more delivery-focused than, an MVP.

**Minimum Viable Product (MVP)** — The smallest version of a product that lets a team test a core assumption with real users and learn from real feedback, rather than the smallest version that's merely technically functional.

## N

**Nexus** — Scrum.org's own scaling framework for 3-9 Scrum Teams sharing one Product Backlog, adding a single new role (the Nexus Integration Team) and a set of extended events focused on cross-team integration. See [Nexus](05-scaling-frameworks/nexus.md).

**Nexus Integration Team (NIT)** — The one structural role Nexus adds: accountable for ensuring a done, integrated Increment is produced every Sprint across all teams in the Nexus, typically staffed by the PO, a Scrum Master, and representative Developers rather than a separate dedicated team.

## O

**Openness (Scrum value)** — One of Scrum's five values: the Scrum Team and its stakeholders are open about the work and the challenges with performing the work, a precondition for transparency to mean anything in practice.

## P

**PI Planning** — SAFe's large, typically two-day, face-to-face event where every team on an Agile Release Train plans the upcoming Program Increment together, surfacing cross-team dependencies on a shared program board. See [SAFe Overview](05-scaling-frameworks/safe-overview.md).

**Planning Poker** — A consensus-based estimation technique where team members simultaneously reveal numeric estimate cards (often Fibonacci-like: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13...) to avoid anchoring on the first number spoken aloud.

**Product Backlog** — The single, ordered, ever-evolving list of everything known to be needed in the product, owned by the Product Owner and refined continuously; the only source of requirements work for the Scrum Team.

**Product Backlog Item (PBI)** — A single entry on the Product Backlog — a feature, a bug fix, a technical improvement, anything that might become part of a future Increment.

**Product Goal** — The long-term objective the Scrum Team is working toward, which the Product Backlog exists to serve; a Scrum Team works on one Product Goal at a time, and each Sprint Goal is a step toward it.

**Product Owner** — The Scrum Team accountable for maximizing the value of the product resulting from the work of the Scrum Team, primarily by effectively managing the Product Backlog. See [The Scrum Team](02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md).

**Program Increment (PI)** — SAFe's fixed timebox, typically 8-12 weeks, during which an Agile Release Train delivers incremental value toward a shared set of PI Objectives — the level above a Sprint. See [SAFe Overview](05-scaling-frameworks/safe-overview.md).

## R

**Refinement** — See Backlog Refinement.

**Release** — The act of making a product Increment available to actual users; in Scrum, a release can happen at any point during or at the end of a Sprint and is a business decision separate from the Sprint's completion.

**Respect (Scrum value)** — One of Scrum's five values: Scrum Team members respect each other to be capable, independent people, a foundation for genuine self-management rather than management by directive.

**Retrospective (Sprint Retrospective)** — The Scrum event closing out each Sprint, where the Scrum Team inspects how the last Sprint went (people, relationships, process, tools) and plans concrete improvements to carry into the next one.

## S

**SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework)** — The most prescriptive and most widely adopted large-enterprise scaling framework, published in four configurations (Essential, Large Solution, Portfolio, Full) built around Agile Release Trains and Program Increments. See [SAFe Overview](05-scaling-frameworks/safe-overview.md) and the certification guides for [SAFe Scrum Master](07-certifications/safe/safe-scrum-master.md) and [SAFe Advanced Scrum Master](07-certifications/safe/safe-advanced-scrum-master.md).

**Scrum** — A lightweight framework, defined in the Scrum Guide, for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products through small teams, fixed-length Sprints, and empirical inspection and adaptation.

**Scrum Guide** — The short (roughly 13-page), deliberately minimal document co-authored by Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland that formally defines Scrum's roles, events, artifacts, and rules; last revised in 2020.

**Scrum Master** — The Scrum Team accountability for establishing Scrum, coaching the team and organization, and removing impediments — a servant-leadership role, not a project-management or people-management role. See [The Scrum Team](02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md).

**Scrum of Scrums** — The foundational coordination unit in Scrum@Scale: a team of team representatives that meets in a Daily-Scrum-like pattern to surface cross-team impediments and dependencies, recursively repeatable at larger scale. See [Scrum@Scale](05-scaling-frameworks/scrum-at-scale.md).

**Scrum Team** — The fundamental unit of Scrum: a Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers, small enough to stay nimble and cross-functional enough to deliver a done Increment without outside dependencies. See [The Scrum Team](02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md).

**Scrum@Scale** — Jeff Sutherland's scaling framework, built on a "scale-free" architecture where the same Scrum of Scrums pattern repeats recursively at every organizational level, deliberately less prescriptive than Nexus or SAFe. See [Scrum@Scale](05-scaling-frameworks/scrum-at-scale.md).

**Self-Managing Team** — The 2020 Scrum Guide's term (replacing "self-organizing") for a team that decides internally who does what, when, and how, without being directed by anyone outside the team.

**Spike** — A short, timeboxed piece of investigative or exploratory work (a technical prototype, a research task) used to reduce uncertainty before committing to a full Product Backlog item.

**Sprint** — The fixed-length, one-month-or-less container for all other Scrum events, during which a done, usable, potentially releasable Increment is created; Sprints happen back to back with no gap between them.

**Sprint Backlog** — The Sprint Goal plus the set of Product Backlog items selected for the Sprint plus the Developers' actionable plan for delivering them — owned entirely by the Developers.

**Sprint Goal** — The single objective set for the Sprint, providing coherence and focus, and giving the Developers flexibility on the exact scope needed to achieve it.

**Sprint Planning** — The event opening a Sprint, where the Scrum Team collaboratively defines the Sprint Goal, selects Product Backlog items, and plans how the selected work will get done.

**Sprint Review** — The event closing out most of a Sprint (before the Retrospective), where the Scrum Team presents the Increment to stakeholders, inspects outcomes, and adapts the Product Backlog based on what's learned.

**Stakeholder** — Anyone outside the Scrum Team with an interest in the product — a customer, an executive sponsor, a dependent team — who provides input, especially at the Sprint Review, but does not direct the Scrum Team's internal work.

**Story Point** — A relative, team-specific unit of estimation representing the effort, complexity, and uncertainty of a Product Backlog item — deliberately not a standardized unit of time, and not meaningfully comparable across different teams.

## T

**Team Topologies** — Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais's vocabulary for organizational team design (stream-aligned, platform, enabling, complicated-subsystem teams), widely used alongside scaling frameworks to decide team boundaries before applying Conway's Law deliberately. See [Why and When to Scale](05-scaling-frameworks/why-and-when-to-scale.md).

**Technical Debt** — The implied future cost of choosing an easy or fast solution now instead of a better, more thorough approach — like financial debt, it accrues "interest" in the form of slower future delivery if left unaddressed.

**Throughput** — The number of work items a team completes per unit of time (e.g., items per week) — a flow metric complementary to cycle time, useful for forecasting without relying on story-point velocity.

**Timebox** — A fixed, non-extendable maximum duration set for an activity or event (a Sprint, a Daily Scrum, a Retrospective) — timeboxing forces regular decision points and bounds the cost of being wrong about a plan.

**Transparency** — The first pillar of empiricism in Scrum: the process and work must be visible to those responsible for the outcome, using a common, shared understanding of terms like "done."

## U

**User Story** — A short, plain-language description of a feature from the perspective of the person who wants it, commonly formatted "As a [role], I want [goal], so that [benefit]" — a popular way to write Product Backlog items, though not a Scrum Guide requirement.

## V

**Value Stream** — The full sequence of activities an organization uses to deliver value to a customer, from initial request to delivery — the unit SAFe's Portfolio-level Lean Budgets are organized around.

**Velocity** — The average amount of work (usually in story points) a team completes per Sprint, used internally for that team's own forecasting — not a productivity score, and not valid for comparison between different teams.

## W

**Waterfall** — A sequential, phase-gated development approach (requirements, then design, then build, then test, then release) that Agile approaches, including Scrum, were largely created in reaction against, due to how late it surfaces problems.

**WIP (Work in Progress)** — The set of work items currently started but not yet finished — a central quantity in flow-based measurement, since high WIP relative to a team's capacity is strongly correlated with longer cycle times.

**WIP Limit** — An explicit cap on how many work items may be in a given workflow state at once, used in Kanban (and sometimes inside Sprints) to force finishing over starting and expose bottlenecks rather than hide them behind everyone looking "busy."
