# Professional Scrum Competencies: The Skill Behind the Framework

*Why Scrum.org built a competency model on top of the Scrum Guide, and how the six areas fit together.*

The Scrum Guide is deliberately short. Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland have trimmed it over every revision, not padded it, and the 2020 version runs about thirteen pages. You can read the whole thing in under an hour and correctly answer "what is a Sprint Review for" on a quiz the same afternoon. That is by design — Scrum's rules are meant to be learnable fast so a team can start using them fast.

But learnable fast and masterable fast are not the same thing. Ask anyone who has run a real Sprint Review with an angry stakeholder in the room, or tried to coach a team out of estimating everything in exact hours, or had to explain to a VP why splitting the team's testers into a shared "QA pool" just broke their ability to finish anything — the rules were never the hard part. The hard part is the judgment: knowing which lever to pull, when, with whom, and how to do it in a way that leaves the team more capable next Sprint than it was this one. That judgment is a skill space, and until Scrum.org named it, it lived only in the heads of experienced practitioners, passed down informally or learned the slow way through failure.

The Professional Scrum Competencies model is Scrum.org's attempt to name that skill space explicitly. It does not add new rules to Scrum — nothing in it contradicts or extends the Scrum Guide. Instead it describes the surrounding craft: the facilitation, coaching, product thinking, technical judgment, organizational awareness, and flow literacy that separate someone who can recite the Scrum Guide from someone who can actually make Scrum work inside a specific, messy, political, historically-dysfunctional organization. If the Scrum Guide is the rulebook, this model is closer to a description of what a skilled player actually does with those rules over a season.

## How this differs from "just knowing the Scrum Guide"

Section [02 — The Scrum Framework](../02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md) in this guide covers the Scrum Guide itself: the accountabilities, the five events, the three artifacts, the commitments, the five values. That is necessary and it is not sufficient. Two Scrum Masters can both correctly describe the purpose of Sprint Planning, and one of them will run a Sprint Planning that produces a shared, motivating Sprint Goal in forty-five minutes while the other runs one that limps to two hours and produces a task list nobody believes in. The difference is not framework knowledge. It's competency — applied skill in facilitation, in reading the room, in knowing how much structure a young team needs versus how much a mature team would find condescending.

The six areas below are where that applied skill lives. Each has its own file in this section with real technique, not just definitions.

## The six competency areas

| Area | What it covers |
|---|---|
| [Understanding and Applying the Scrum Framework](01-understanding-and-applying-scrum-framework.md) | Fluency deep enough to recognize a theory violation under pressure and defend Scrum's integrity without becoming dogmatic about it. |
| [Developing People and Teams](02-developing-people-and-teams.md) | Growing individuals and building genuinely self-managing, cross-functional teams through facilitation, situational leadership, coaching, mentoring, and teaching. |
| [Managing Products with Agility](03-managing-products-with-agility.md) | Forecasting, product vision, value, backlog management, business strategy, and stakeholder engagement — Product Owner territory the Scrum Master enables. |
| [Developing and Delivering Products Professionally](04-developing-and-delivering-products-professionally.md) | The engineering-adjacent judgment needed to keep "Done" honest: emergent design and technical risk management. |
| [Evolving the Agile Organization](05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md) | Organizational design, culture change, portfolio planning, and Evidence-Based Management at the level above a single team. |
| [Optimizing Flow](06-optimizing-flow.md) | Making delivery flow transparent and improving it with Kanban-style thinking, metrics, and automation, especially where work continues beyond the Sprint boundary. |

Notice what is not on this list: nothing here is "run the Daily Scrum" or "know what a Sprint Backlog is." Those are Scrum Guide mechanics, covered in [section 02](../02-scrum-framework/scrum-team.md). This model assumes you already have that and asks a harder question: given that you know the rules, can you actually make a team, a product, and an organization better with them?

## Why this matters for certification, even when the exam doesn't name it

Scrum.org's PSM I is mostly a Scrum Guide recall-and-application test — hard, but bounded by the Guide's own thirteen pages. [PSM II](../07-certifications/scrum-org/psm-ii.md) and [PSM III](../07-certifications/scrum-org/psm-iii.md) are different. Their scenario-based questions routinely require exactly the judgment this competency model describes — distinguishing a legitimate adaptation from a rules violation, choosing a coaching stance over a directive one, reading an organizational impediment behind a team-level symptom — without ever citing "Professional Scrum Competencies" as the source. Scrum Alliance's CSP-SM path is even more explicit about it: its learning objectives were written to mirror this same competency space, just organized under different section names.

That's the practical reason this guide gives the model its own section rather than folding it into a glossary entry. If you're only studying the Scrum Guide's text, you can pass PSM I and still get blindsided by a PSM II scenario question that has no single "correct rule" answer — it has a best judgment call, and the six areas here are what your judgment is being measured against. Read this section closely regardless of which certification you're aiming for; it is the closest thing the industry has to a shared syllabus across Scrum.org, Scrum Alliance, and — less formally — the SAFe Scrum Master tracks covered in [section 05](../05-scaling-frameworks/why-and-when-to-scale.md).

Start with [Understanding and Applying the Scrum Framework](01-understanding-and-applying-scrum-framework.md) — everything else in this section assumes that foundational fluency and builds outward from it.
