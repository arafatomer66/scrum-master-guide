# Managing Products with Agility

*Forecasting, vision, value, backlog management, business strategy, and stakeholders — Product Owner territory that a Scrum Master enables rather than owns.*

This competency area belongs to the Product Owner. The Scrum Guide is explicit that Product Backlog ordering, product vision, and value decisions sit with one accountable person, and a Scrum Master who starts making those calls has quietly taken over someone else's job — a violation of exactly the kind covered in [Understanding and Applying the Scrum Framework](01-understanding-and-applying-scrum-framework.md). But "not owning it" doesn't mean "irrelevant to it." A Scrum Master who understands product management well enough to ask a sharp question, spot a weak vision statement, or notice a backlog that's really a feature wishlist is a far more useful partner to a Product Owner than one who treats the Product Backlog as someone else's spreadsheet. This file covers the six focus areas Scrum.org names here, each with the technique behind it and where the Scrum Master's facilitation role actually fits.

## Forecasting and Release Planning

The simplest forecasting method is velocity-based: take a team's recent throughput — story points or item count completed per Sprint over the last several Sprints — and divide the remaining backlog by that average to estimate how many Sprints are left. It's easy to compute and easy to explain to a stakeholder, but it produces a single number that hides the real uncertainty in the data; a team whose velocity ranged from 18 to 34 points over the last six Sprints doesn't actually know if a 200-point backlog finishes in six Sprints or ten.

Monte Carlo forecasting addresses that by treating the historical variation as the forecast rather than averaging it away. Conceptually: take the team's actual history of Sprint-by-Sprint throughput (or, in flow-based settings, cycle time per item — see [Optimizing Flow](06-optimizing-flow.md)), and run thousands of simulated "what if the next N Sprints looked like a random draw from our own history" trials. The output isn't one date, it's a probability distribution — "there's an 85% chance we finish by Sprint 9, a 50% chance by Sprint 7." That's a materially more honest answer to give a stakeholder asking "when will this be done," and it's the forecasting approach covered in depth at [metrics and forecasting](../04-scrum-master-role/metrics-and-forecasting.md). A Scrum Master's role here is rarely running the simulation personally — it's coaching the Product Owner toward probabilistic language and away from the false confidence of a single committed date.

## Product Vision

A strong product vision is not a mission statement or a slogan. It names a real customer problem worth solving, stays stable across quarters even as the backlog beneath it churns weekly, and is falsifiable — you can imagine evidence that would prove it wrong, which is what makes it testable through actual delivery rather than just aspirational. A vision like "be the best platform for X" fails that test; nothing could disconfirm it. A vision like "cut the time a small retailer spends reconciling inventory from hours to minutes" gives every Sprint Review something concrete to check the increment against. A Scrum Master who notices a Product Backlog with no discoverable vision behind it — just a queue of tickets — has spotted a real problem worth raising, even though writing the vision itself is the Product Owner's call.

## Product Value

Value is defined by outcomes, not output — a shipped feature that nobody uses is zero value regardless of how much effort it took. Measuring it requires both leading indicators (adoption rate of a new feature in its first two weeks, task-completion time in a usability test) and lagging indicators (retention, revenue, support-ticket volume). This is the same territory formalized by Evidence-Based Management, covered fully under [Evolving the Agile Organization](05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md) — EBM's Current Value and Unrealized Value measures exist precisely so value conversations have evidence behind them instead of opinion.

## Product Backlog Management

Good refinement technique follows the DEEP heuristic: the backlog is **detailed appropriately** (near-term items sliced small and clear, distant ones deliberately coarse), **estimated** (at whatever granularity the team finds useful), **emergent** (expected to change as the team and market learn more, not treated as a fixed contract), and **prioritized** (a strict single order, not a set of equally-important tiers). The most common failure a Scrum Master will see is refinement happening too early or too thoroughly — a "Definition of Ready" enforced so rigidly that it becomes a second Definition of Done and slows the backlog to a crawl. Vertical slicing — splitting a feature so each slice delivers a thin but complete piece of user-visible value, rather than splitting by technical layer — is the core splitting technique worth coaching a Product Owner and Developers through together.

## Business Strategy

A healthy Product Backlog traces upward: each significant item should connect to a stated business objective, not exist because a stakeholder asked for it once in a hallway. This is where product management meets company strategy — OKRs, quarterly business goals, competitive positioning — and it's also where a lot of backlogs quietly go wrong, accumulating items that made sense in isolation but collectively pull the product in no coherent direction. A Scrum Master won't set business strategy, but can usefully ask, in backlog refinement, "which objective does this serve?" often enough that the habit of asking spreads to the team itself.

## Stakeholders and Customers

This is the focus area where the Scrum Master's facilitation role is most direct, even though the underlying accountability stays with the Product Owner. A Sprint Review that's become a scripted demo followed by polite applause has stopped being an inspection event — the Scrum Master's job is to notice that drift and redesign the format so stakeholders are actually giving feedback that changes the backlog, not just watching a status update. Equally important is protecting the team's focus: fielding a stakeholder who wants to inject urgent work mid-Sprint by redirecting them to the Product Owner and the next Sprint Planning, rather than letting scope quietly balloon inside a Sprint that's supposedly already committed. The Scrum Master also often coaches the Product Owner directly on stakeholder technique — how to run a Review that invites real challenge, how to say no to a request without burning the relationship, how to keep multiple stakeholders' competing priorities visible on one ordered backlog instead of maintaining parallel unofficial lists to keep everyone happy.

Every one of these six areas ultimately depends on the team being able to actually build what the Product Owner and stakeholders agree matters — which is where [Developing and Delivering Products Professionally](04-developing-and-delivering-products-professionally.md) picks up, and where product agility meets engineering reality.
