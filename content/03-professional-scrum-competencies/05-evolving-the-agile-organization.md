# Evolving the Agile Organization

*Organizational design, culture change, portfolio planning, and Evidence-Based Management — the org-level work of removing impediments no single team can remove itself.*

Every other competency in this section assumes a team operating inside an organization that's basically compatible with Scrum. This one is for when that assumption breaks — when the impediment isn't inside the team's own habits at all, but baked into how the company is structured, funded, or led. A Scrum Master who only ever works at the team level will eventually hit a ceiling: the team is doing everything right, and it's still slow, still blocked, still delivering less value than it should, because the organization around it wasn't built for this way of working. This competency is about seeing that clearly and having language and evidence to do something about it — which is also why it's the area most heavily tested at [PSM III](../07-certifications/scrum-org/psm-iii.md), Scrum.org's most senior credential.

## Organizational design

Most companies are organized around functional silos — a frontend team, a backend team, a QA team, a DBA team — because that's how work was staffed before anyone was trying to ship small increments continuously. That structure creates a specific, predictable failure mode for Scrum teams: a cross-functional Sprint Backlog item needs three different silos to sign off, and none of those silos report to anyone the team can actually influence, so "cross-functional team" stays aspirational no matter how the team's own charter reads. This is why organizational design keeps surfacing as an impediment even in companies that have fully adopted Scrum's events and artifacts.

A useful conceptual lens here — not a Scrum Guide concept, but one that shows up constantly in how mature organizations restructure around this problem — is team topologies: distinguishing a **stream-aligned team** (owns a slice of product value end to end, which is what a healthy Scrum Team should be), a **platform team** (builds shared infrastructure that stream-aligned teams consume self-service, without becoming a bottleneck those teams have to queue behind), an **enabling team** (temporarily helps a stream-aligned team build a missing capability, then leaves), and a **complicated-subsystem team** (owns a piece of deep specialist knowledge, like a pricing engine, that genuinely can't be spread across every team). Most organizational dysfunction a Scrum Master encounters traces back to a team that should be stream-aligned instead being trapped as a shared resource pool — the DBA team six Scrum Teams all depend on and none of them control is the single most common concrete example. Naming this structure to leadership, in terms they recognize from their own org chart, is often more persuasive than any amount of Scrum theory.

## Culture change: doing agile vs. being agile

An organization can perform every Scrum event flawlessly and still not be agile in any way that matters. The tell is usually a mismatch between the ritual and its purpose: a Daily Scrum that happens on schedule every day, fifteen minutes, three questions answered in order — except it's actually a status report delivered upward to a manager standing in the room, and nothing about how the team plans its next 24 hours changes based on what gets said. A Sprint Review that happens every two weeks on the calendar — except stakeholders were told the scope weeks ago and nothing they say in the room has ever changed a subsequent Sprint. A Retrospective that produces an action item every single time — except it's the same action item as three Sprints ago, because nobody with authority to fix the underlying cause was in the room. Doing agile is the theater; being agile is the events actually functioning as the inspect-and-adapt mechanism they were designed to be. A Scrum Master's job in this focus area is diagnosing which one they're looking at — the calendar alone can't tell you, you have to watch whether anything actually changes as a result of the event.

## Portfolio planning at scale

Once an organization runs more than a handful of Scrum Teams, someone has to decide which initiatives get funded and staffed across all of them, and how that portfolio gets sequenced and re-sequenced as the market changes. Lean portfolio management approaches this the same way a single Product Backlog is managed — a prioritized, transparent, regularly re-ordered list of initiatives rather than an annual fixed roadmap — just at a scale spanning many teams and often many quarters. This is where scaling frameworks like SAFe, LeSS, Nexus, and Scrum@Scale become directly relevant, and it's covered in full at [why and when to scale](../05-scaling-frameworks/why-and-when-to-scale.md); the short version here is that portfolio-level agility is what lets multiple Scrum Teams stay aligned to real business priority without a top-down annual plan quietly overriding every team's Sprint-level empiricism.

## Evidence-Based Management

Evidence-Based Management (EBM) is Scrum.org's framework for making organizational value conversations empirical instead of opinion-driven, using four key value areas:

**Current Value (CV)** measures the value the product delivers to customers and the organization right now — revenue, customer satisfaction, usage. **Unrealized Value (UV)** measures the gap between what the product currently delivers and what it could deliver if it fully met customer and market needs — the size of the opportunity still on the table. **Time-to-Market (T2M)** measures how quickly the organization can turn an idea into working functionality in customers' hands — cycle time from concept to release, release frequency, lead time for a change. **Ability to Innovate (A2I)** measures how much capacity the organization is actually spending building new value versus keeping the lights on — the share of effort going to defects, technical debt, and maintenance versus new capability.

These four areas matter to a Scrum Master specifically because they turn a vague complaint into evidence leadership can act on. "The team feels blocked a lot" doesn't move a budget conversation. "Our A2I data shows 60% of Developer capacity over the last two quarters went to defect fixes and manual deployment steps, not new features" does — it's the same underlying problem, converted from a feeling into a number an executive can put in front of their own leadership. Using EBM this way is what makes organizational impediments visible enough to actually get resourced, rather than staying a permanent, unaddressed complaint at every Retrospective.

Organizational change of this kind rarely happens through a single Sprint Team's effort alone — it depends on making the surrounding delivery pipeline visible and measurable too, which is exactly the territory [Optimizing Flow](06-optimizing-flow.md) covers next.
