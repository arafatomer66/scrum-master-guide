# Developing People and Teams

*Self-managing teams, facilitation, situational leadership, coaching, and mentoring — how a Scrum Master grows individuals and builds a team that can solve hard problems on its own.*

If [Understanding and Applying the Scrum Framework](01-understanding-and-applying-scrum-framework.md) is about the Scrum Master's relationship to the rules, this competency is about their relationship to people, and it's where most of a Scrum Master's actual week gets spent. Scrum.org groups five focus areas under it: Self-Managing Teams, Facilitation, Leadership Styles, Coaching, and Mentoring and Teaching. They overlap in practice — you facilitate a conversation using a coaching stance to grow a team's self-management — but each names a distinct skill worth building deliberately.

## Self-Managing Teams

"Self-managing" replaced "self-organizing" in the 2020 Scrum Guide for a reason: it's more specific about what the team actually controls. A self-managing team chooses *who* does the work, *how* to do it, and *what* to work on within the Sprint Goal — without someone outside the team making those calls for them. That sounds like a mindset, but it's really a structural condition, and a Scrum Master's job is to check the structure, not just encourage the mindset.

Concretely, self-management requires: the team has the skills to complete work without waiting on people outside it (true cross-functionality — no permanent dependency on a shared DBA team or an external test group); the team has genuine authority over its own process (nobody outside the team is assigning tickets, dictating standup format, or overriding sprint scope mid-Sprint); membership is stable enough that trust and shared context can accumulate instead of resetting every few weeks; and the team has direct access to the people who can answer its questions — stakeholders, customers, architects — rather than routing every question through a manager. When self-management isn't happening, the first place to look is rarely "the team needs a pep talk." It's usually one of these four structural conditions being quietly absent, often for organizational reasons well above the team's pay grade — which is exactly the kind of impediment covered under [Evolving the Agile Organization](05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md).

## Facilitation

Facilitation is the most visible, most practiced skill in the role, and it deserves its own deep-dive at [facilitation techniques](../04-scrum-master-role/facilitation-techniques.md) — this is the conceptual grounding for that. The core discipline is staying neutral on content while actively shaping process: a facilitator doesn't argue for a particular Sprint Goal, but does actively manage how the group arrives at one.

A working toolkit includes: **silent brainstorming**, where participants write ideas individually before any group discussion, preventing the loudest voice from anchoring everyone else's thinking; **dot voting**, for converging a long list down to a workable few without a debate that eats the whole timebox; **round-robin**, going person by person so quieter team members aren't perpetually talked over in open-floor discussion; **fist of five**, a fast consensus-check where everyone shows zero to five fingers to signal agreement level, letting a facilitator spot disagreement instantly instead of assuming silence means consent; the **parking lot**, a visible place to capture off-topic but valid points so the group can stay on track without dismissing them; and structures like **1-2-4-all** (individual thought, then pairs, then groups of four, then the whole room), which reliably surfaces more perspectives in less time than open discussion. None of these are exotic — they're chosen because each solves a specific failure mode of group conversation, and a competent Scrum Master picks the technique to match the failure mode they're seeing, not out of habit.

## Leadership Styles

Not every team needs the same kind of Scrum Master input, and the situational leadership model — directing, coaching, supporting, delegating — gives useful language for matching style to a team's maturity.

A **directing** style, giving specific instructions and checking closely, fits a brand-new team that doesn't yet know what a Sprint Goal is or how Scrum events connect — early on, more structure genuinely helps rather than undermines self-management, because the team hasn't yet learned enough to self-manage effectively. A **coaching** style, still fairly directive but now explaining the why and inviting input, fits a team that understands the mechanics but hasn't internalized the reasoning yet. A **supporting** style, where the Scrum Master facilitates and encourages but the team drives decisions, fits a team that understands Scrum and mostly just needs someone protecting its space and occasionally asking a sharpening question. A **delegating** style, where the Scrum Master steps back almost entirely and only engages when asked, fits a mature, high-trust team that would find continued hand-holding condescending and counterproductive. The skill is not picking a favorite style — most Scrum Masters have one they're naturally drawn to — but reading which one the team in front of them actually needs this quarter, and being willing to shift down to directing again if a team's composition changes or a crisis resets their footing.

## Coaching

Coaching, in the Professional Scrum sense, is a specific stance: asking rather than telling, holding space for someone to find their own answer rather than supplying yours, and trusting that a person or team who arrives at a solution themselves will own it far more durably than one who was handed it. A Scrum Master coaching a developer who's stuck doesn't say "just talk to the PO about it" — they ask "what's stopping you from raising this with the PO?" and let the real obstacle surface, which is often something the advice would have missed entirely (fear of looking behind, a past bad experience, unclear on whose call it even is). Coaching this section overlaps directly with the fuller treatment in [coaching vs. mentoring vs. teaching](../04-scrum-master-role/coaching-vs-mentoring-vs-teaching.md).

## Mentoring and Teaching

These are close cousins of coaching but structurally different, and conflating them is one of the most common ways new Scrum Masters undercut their own effectiveness. **Teaching** is direct instruction — explaining what a Sprint Review is for to someone who's never seen one, walking a new joiner through the team's Definition of Done, or running a lunch-and-learn on estimation techniques. There's a right answer being transmitted, and the Scrum Master's job is to transmit it clearly. **Mentoring** is sharing your own hard-won experience when it's relevant and invited — telling a newer Scrum Master on a sister team "when I had a stakeholder do that, here's what worked and here's what backfired," offered as one data point, not a prescription. **Coaching**, by contrast, deliberately withholds the answer even when the coach has one, because the goal is the other person's own growth, not an efficient transfer of the coach's opinion.

A realistic week shows all three in play: Monday, teaching a new hire what the Sprint Backlog actually is before their first Planning session; Tuesday, a coaching conversation with a developer wrestling with whether to raise a concern about scope creep, where the Scrum Master asks questions instead of offering a script; Wednesday, facilitating the Daily Scrum using round-robin because the team has been talking over each other; Thursday, mentoring a junior Scrum Master from another team over coffee, sharing what worked the last time a stakeholder tried to bypass the Product Owner; Friday, running the Retrospective with a fist-of-five check to confirm real consensus on the improvement action before the Sprint closes. That mix — not any single skill in isolation — is what this competency is actually describing, and it's the day-to-day texture covered in fuller detail across [The Scrum Master Role](../04-scrum-master-role/facilitation-techniques.md).

Building people and teams this way is what makes the next competency possible: a team that can't manage itself can't reliably [manage a product with agility](03-managing-products-with-agility.md) either, because product agility depends on the team actually owning its own delivery decisions.
