# Developing and Delivering Products Professionally

*Why "Done" software has to emerge instead of being designed upfront, why technical debt is a Scrum Master's problem too, and how to advocate for engineering excellence without making engineering decisions.*

This is the competency area Scrum Masters most often shy away from, usually with some version of "I'm not technical, that's not my lane." That instinct gets the boundary in the wrong place. Deciding which testing framework to use, or whether to adopt trunk-based development, or how to structure a service boundary — those genuinely are Developer decisions, and a Scrum Master who starts making them has crossed into the same overreach covered in [Managing Products with Agility](03-managing-products-with-agility.md). But *whether the team's engineering practices are strong enough to make "Done" mean something* is not a technical question — it's a question about whether empiricism itself is intact, and that is squarely the Scrum Master's business.

## Emergent Software Development

Scrum treats software development as complex work, not merely complicated work — the distinction matters. Complicated problems (building a bridge to a known span, using known materials, following known physics) can be fully designed upfront by an expert, because the relationship between cause and effect is knowable in advance. Complex problems (what will actually satisfy this market, what will this codebase need to support in eighteen months, how will users actually behave once the feature ships) cannot be fully designed upfront, because cause and effect are only knowable in hindsight, after you've tried something and observed what happened. This is the same complexity framing that underlies the empiricism covered in [section 01's agile foundations](../01-agile-foundations/) and [Understanding and Applying the Scrum Framework](01-understanding-and-applying-scrum-framework.md).

Practically, this is why a "Done" product has to emerge Sprint by Sprint rather than being architected in full before a line of code is written. A team that spends the first three Sprints only on upfront design, with nothing releasable to show, has no empirical evidence yet — no real user has touched anything, no assumption has been tested against reality. A team that builds a thin, working vertical slice every Sprint accumulates evidence continuously, and each Sprint's real feedback reshapes what the next Sprint should build. This is also why YAGNI ("you aren't gonna need it") and evolutionary architecture — designing for what's known now while keeping the design cheap to change later — are the natural technical companions to Scrum's process empiricism, even though a Scrum Master doesn't personally choose the architecture. What a Scrum Master can and should watch for is a team quietly reverting to big-design-upfront habits under stakeholder pressure — a symptom worth raising even without opining on the design itself.

## Managing Technical Risk

Technical debt behaves like financial debt: a shortcut taken under Sprint pressure ("we'll skip the edge-case tests, we're out of time") buys speed now and charges compounding interest later, in the form of slower future Sprints, more defects, and features that get harder to build the longer the debt goes unpaid. Left unmanaged, it's the single most common reason a team's velocity quietly declines over a year even though nothing dramatic ever seemed to go wrong in any one Sprint.

The Definition of Done is the framework's primary risk-management tool against exactly this. A DoD that genuinely requires automated tests passing, code reviewed, and the increment integrated and releasable means each Sprint's output is real, evidence-backed progress — the risk of "we thought this was done but it wasn't" gets retired every Sprint instead of accumulating silently until a release date makes it visible all at once, usually at the worst possible time. A DoD that's been quietly watered down — "we'll write the tests next Sprint," repeated for months — is a theory violation of the kind covered in [Understanding and Applying the Scrum Framework](01-understanding-and-applying-scrum-framework.md): the artifact still exists and still gets called Done, but it no longer means what everyone at the Sprint Review assumes it means. Inspecting a "Done" increment that isn't actually done is inspecting a fiction, and every decision made from that fiction — forecasts, stakeholder commitments, the next Sprint's plan — inherits the same false confidence.

This is why a Scrum Master should care about CI/CD pipelines, automated test coverage, code review discipline, and refactoring habits without needing to be able to write the code themselves. These aren't engineering preferences to have an opinion on — they're the mechanism by which "Done" stays honest. A team without automated regression tests can still technically finish Sprints, but every Sprint after the first few gets slower and riskier as manual verification effort grows, and the Scrum Master watching only velocity numbers will see the symptom (slowing throughput) without understanding the cause (accumulating technical risk) unless they know to look for it.

## Advocating without overstepping

The line a Scrum Master needs to hold here is genuinely fine, and it's worth naming explicitly. Overstepping sounds like: "we should be using feature flags" or "you need better test coverage before you can call this Done" — directive statements handed down about a technical solution. Staying in-lane sounds like: "our Definition of Done doesn't currently require automated regression tests — is that a risk worth this team discussing?" or, at Sprint Review, "the increment took two extra days of manual verification this Sprint that it didn't take last Sprint — is that worth raising at the Retrospective?" The Scrum Master surfaces the systemic pattern and asks the question; the Developers, who own the how, decide what to do about it. This is the same coaching stance covered under [Developing People and Teams](02-developing-people-and-teams.md) applied specifically to technical practice — asking rather than telling, trusting that a team who chooses its own engineering discipline will actually sustain it, where discipline imposed from outside rarely survives the next deadline crunch.

[PSM II](../07-certifications/scrum-org/psm-ii.md) tests this competency directly, usually through scenarios where a team is hitting its Sprint Goal on paper while technical debt quietly builds — the exam wants you to recognize the risk and choose the coaching response over the directive one. It's also the bridge to the next competency: technical risk that a single team can't resolve on its own — a shared platform team with no capacity, an organization-wide ban on automated deployment tooling — becomes an organizational impediment, which is where [Evolving the Agile Organization](05-evolving-the-agile-organization.md) picks up.
