# Agile vs. Waterfall

*A fair comparison of sequential and iterative delivery — the cost-of-change curve that explains why software moved away from Waterfall, and the real situations where Waterfall still legitimately wins.*

Waterfall gets treated as a punching bag in most Agile material, which is a mistake for two reasons: it makes the argument for Agile weaker, not stronger, because it's arguing against a strawman instead of the actual thing; and it hides the fact that Waterfall is still the correct choice in specific, identifiable situations. Understanding why requires understanding what Waterfall actually is, not the caricature of it.

## What Waterfall actually is

Waterfall, formalized by Winston Royce's 1970 paper "Managing the Development of Large Software Systems" (ironically, Royce's own paper argued against using it in the strictly sequential form it became famous for), is a phase-gated approach: requirements are gathered and frozen, then design is completed and reviewed, then implementation happens against that fixed design, then testing verifies the implementation against the original requirements, then the system deploys. Each phase has a formal exit gate — usually a sign-off document — before the next phase begins. Going backward is possible but expensive and often requires re-approval from whoever signed off on the earlier phase.

This isn't stupid. It's a rational model when you can front-load nearly all the uncertainty into the requirements and design phases and be confident those decisions will still be correct by the time you build and ship. The entire model is a bet that requirements analysis, done thoroughly enough up front, is cheaper than discovering requirements through iteration. Sometimes that bet is right.

## The cost-of-change curve

This is the concept that actually explains the shift to Agile, and it's an empirical claim, not a philosophical one. Barry Boehm's research on software economics (documented across his work from the 1970s through *Software Cost Estimation with COCOMO II*, 2000) found that the cost to fix a defect or change a requirement rises sharply the later it's discovered in a sequential process — commonly cited as somewhere between 10x and 100x more expensive to fix in production than during requirements, though the exact multiplier varies by project and has been debated since. The mechanism is straightforward: a wrong requirement caught during requirements analysis costs a conversation. The same wrong requirement, undiscovered until after implementation, testing, and deployment, costs redesign, rework across everything built on top of the wrong assumption, retesting, and a production fix — plus whatever damage already happened while it was live and wrong.

Waterfall's structural bet is that you can catch nearly all requirement and design errors before the expensive phases begin, by doing requirements and design thoroughly enough up front. When that bet holds — stable, well-understood domains — the curve doesn't hurt you much, because you rarely climb it. When the bet fails — and in most software, it fails constantly, because users don't actually know what they want until they see something real, and markets shift mid-project — you end up discovering the expensive kind of change anyway, just later, when it costs the most.

Iterative delivery's answer to the same curve isn't to eliminate it — it's to shorten the distance between "a decision is made" and "the decision gets tested against reality," so that when you do climb the curve, you climb it in small, cheap increments (a two-week Sprint's worth of assumptions) instead of one enormous, expensive one (a year's worth). This is the direct economic argument for why a Scrum Sprint is short and produces a working Increment every time, rather than a partial artifact that can't be validated: an Increment collapses the distance between decision and feedback down to weeks, which is the whole mechanism by which Scrum keeps the cost-of-change curve flat instead of steep.

## Where Waterfall still legitimately wins

This is the part most Agile advocacy skips, and skipping it is intellectually dishonest.

**Regulated hardware and safety-critical systems.** Medical device firmware regulated under IEC 62304, avionics software under DO-178C, or automotive safety systems under ISO 26262 operate under certification regimes that require traceable, documented requirements before implementation, with formal verification against those exact requirements. You cannot iteratively discover requirements for a pacemaker's firmware the way you'd iteratively discover a checkout flow's UX — the cost of a late-discovered defect isn't rework, it's a recall or a fatality, and the certification process itself is structured around sequential, documented gates because regulators need an auditable trail. Agile methods exist inside these industries (there are hybrid models combining Scrum inside a regulated Waterfall-shaped envelope), but the outer structure is Waterfall-shaped for good reason.

**Fixed-price contracts with genuinely rigid scope.** If a client has legally committed to a fixed price for a fixed, fully specified scope — common in government procurement and some construction-adjacent software work — there's often no room for the kind of scope renegotiation Agile assumes is healthy. Discovering mid-project that the specified scope was wrong isn't an opportunity to adapt, it's a contract dispute. In these cases, the discipline of getting requirements right before signing is doing real economic work, and Agile's "welcome changing requirements" principle runs directly into a contract structure that punishes exactly that.

**Domains where the cost-of-change curve is genuinely steep regardless of process.** Large civil engineering projects, aerospace hardware, and anything involving physical manufacturing tooling have a cost-of-change curve that's steep for physical reasons software doesn't share — you can't "refactor" a poured foundation or a stamped chassis mold the way you refactor code. Software's curve is comparatively flat because the artifact itself is cheap to change; this is a genuine, structural difference between domains, not a difference in how disciplined the teams are.

**Genuinely well-understood, low-uncertainty work.** A team building the fifth near-identical implementation of a well-understood system (a standard payroll integration, a known compliance report) has low requirements uncertainty. Iterating on something you already understand thoroughly adds process overhead without buying much discovery, because there's little left to discover.

## Why most software teams moved away from it anyway

Most software isn't in any of those categories. Product software is built against markets and users whose needs aren't fully knowable in advance, and the cost of building the wrong thing is opportunity cost and rework, not physical rework of poured concrete or bodily harm — meaning the cost-of-change curve genuinely can be kept flat by iterating. In that environment, Waterfall's core bet — that you can specify requirements correctly enough up front to skip iteration — usually loses, because the requirements themselves are unknown until users interact with something real. That's the actual argument for Agile: not that planning is bad, but that in high-uncertainty domains, a plan you can't cheaply revise is a liability, and iterative delivery is a direct structural response to that specific economic reality — grounded in the empirical process control theory covered in [Empiricism and Lean Thinking](empiricism-and-lean-thinking.md).

A Scrum Master should be able to make this argument to a skeptical stakeholder without strawmanning Waterfall, because a stakeholder who's seen Waterfall work somewhere (a regulated industry, a fixed-price vendor contract) will correctly smell a strawman and stop trusting the rest of the pitch. The honest version — "here's the actual bet Waterfall makes, here's why that bet doesn't hold for what we're building, here's the evidence" — is both more persuasive and more accurate.
